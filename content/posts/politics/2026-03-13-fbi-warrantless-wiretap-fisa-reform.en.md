A <span class="hl-blue">bipartisan bill</span> in Congress would force the FBI to obtain a warrant before reading Americans' private digital messages — targeting one of the most expansive warrantless surveillance programs in the country's history, with a hard deadline approaching in <mark>April 2026</mark>.

## Key Takeaways

- The legislation targets **FISA Section 702**, under which the FBI conducted over <mark>280,000</mark> warrantless searches of Americans' communications data in a single year
- Current Section 702 authorization expires in <mark>April 2026</mark>, creating a narrow and concrete legislative window for reform
- The bill would also ban federal agencies from purchasing commercial data on US residents — closing a major surveillance back-channel that currently requires no warrant

## What Is FISA Section 702?

**FISA Section 702** — a provision of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act — authorizes warrantless surveillance of foreign persons located outside the United States. The NSA, CIA, FBI, and other agencies use this authority to intercept communications of foreign intelligence targets by compelling US technology companies to provide access.

The constitutional complication: when a foreign target communicates with an American citizen, the American's communications are also collected. This **"incidental collection"** ends up in a searchable database. And the FBI can query that database — to read an American's private messages — without obtaining a warrant.

A <mark>2022</mark> FISA Court opinion disclosed the FBI conducted over <span class="hl-red">280,000 warrantless searches</span> of Americans' data in a single year using Section 702 collection. The targets ranged from national security investigations to ordinary domestic criminal cases — far broader than Section 702's original foreign intelligence framing.

> "Section 702 was sold to Congress as a foreign intelligence tool. In practice, it became a domestic surveillance database the FBI can query without a warrant whenever it finds a connection to a foreign target."

## The Bipartisan Moment

What makes this legislative window unusual is the coalition assembled around reform. Civil liberties reform has historically aligned with progressive Democrats concerned about government overreach. Today it includes a significant faction of Republicans aligned with conservative distrust of federal law enforcement.

<span class="hl-blue">Supporters</span> of the warrant requirement:
- Civil liberties organizations (ACLU, Electronic Frontier Foundation)
- Privacy-focused conservatives and libertarians
- Technology companies with user trust concerns
- Members of both parties representing constituents wary of federal surveillance

<span class="hl-red">Opponents</span>:
- Intelligence community agencies (NSA, CIA) and their congressional allies
- National security hawks in both parties
- DOJ and FBI, arguing warrant requirements add operationally damaging friction to time-sensitive national security investigations

The reform coalition's counterargument: the documented history of querying 702 data in non-national-security criminal investigations demonstrates the tool has already escaped its intended scope, and warrants are the appropriate constitutional check.

## The Commercial Data Loophole

The bill's second major provision — banning federal purchase of commercial data on US residents — targets the **"data broker loophole."** Federal agencies have routinely purchased location data, browsing history, and other personal information from commercial data brokers. Because consumers nominally "agreed" to data collection in app terms of service, agencies argue purchasing this data is a commercial transaction — not a government seizure — and requires no warrant.

<span class="hl-red">The practical effect:</span> the government can construct detailed surveillance profiles of Americans — movements, digital behaviors, social connections — without a warrant, by simply purchasing data that commercial companies have already aggregated. The **Carpenter v. United States** (2018) Supreme Court ruling required warrants for extended cell-site location history but left commercial data purchases in a legal gray zone this bill would close legislatively.

## The April Clock

| Scenario | Outcome |
|----------|--------|
| Clean reauthorization | Intelligence community wins; status quo preserved |
| Reauthorization with warrant requirement | Civil liberties win |
| Partial reform | Negotiated compromise |
| No reauthorization by April | 702 lapses; intelligence disruption, emergency legislation |

The expiration deadline is the reform coalition's primary leverage. Intelligence agencies strongly prefer clean reauthorization. But allowing Section 702 to lapse — disrupting ongoing operations — is a scenario the national security community wants to avoid even more than accepting reform.

The outcome of this narrow window will determine whether a warrant retains its constitutional meaning in the era of ambient digital surveillance — and whether Congress can act on the rare moment when left and right, for different reasons, want the same constraint on federal power.
