Trump announced he will postpone his planned visit to China with President Xi, citing his need to focus on the Iran war response. According to BBC and Yonhap News Agency, Trump indicated that a China visit could be possible "<mark>in about 5 or 6 weeks</mark>."

## Key Takeaways

- Trump deprioritizes Xi summit before Iran conflict resolution — signals possible <mark>5–6 week</mark> delay in China visit
- China signals "no rush" — strategy to seize negotiating leverage while the U.S. is tied down by the Iran war
- Across-the-board vacuum in core agenda items: trade talks, semiconductor export controls, and the Taiwan issue

## The Iran War's Impact on US-China Relations

As the military conflict between Iran and Israel/the U.S. escalated in earnest in <mark>March 2026</mark>, the Trump administration's diplomatic resources shifted toward the Middle East. This produces two contrasting effects on US-China relations.

**In the short term**: A vacuum in trade and technology negotiations. Key agenda items that Trump has been pursuing — tariff renegotiations, adjustments to technology export controls targeting Huawei and SMIC, and security cooperation over the Strait of Hormuz — are all left adrift.

**In the medium term**: China's bargaining power grows. While the U.S. is tied down in the Middle East, <span class="hl-blue">China gains breathing room free from tariff pressure</span>. Beijing is assessed to be publicly signaling that it is in no rush in order to seize the upper hand in negotiations.

> "The Iran war is not just a Middle Eastern issue — it is a geopolitical testing ground that will determine how Washington and Beijing deal with each other."

## China's Strategic Calculus

Bloomberg reported that "Trump's delay of the Xi meeting gives China time to analyze the Iran war dynamics." From China's perspective, a Trump-Xi summit is a double-edged sword.

**If the summit goes ahead**: The likelihood of trade war de-escalation increases. However, China would simultaneously face direct U.S. pressure on sensitive issues such as Taiwan, technology, and military affairs.

**If the summit is delayed**: The status quo continues. The current <span class="hl-red">tariff regime</span> is a burden on Chinese exports, but buying time without additional pressure is structurally advantageous. Particularly as China has declared <mark>semiconductor independence by 2030</mark> and is accelerating the buildout of its domestic supply chain, some analysts believe it is strategically more beneficial to focus on strengthening independent technological capabilities rather than negotiating with the U.S.

## Trump: "No Longer Need NATO, South Korea, or Japan Naval Support"

Even more striking was a separate statement by Trump. According to Yonhap News Agency, President Trump stated that "the U.S. no longer needs naval support from NATO allies, South Korea, or Japan." This is interpreted as a signal that the U.S. intends to handle the Middle East conflict unilaterally, but it has simultaneously sent shockwaves through the East Asian diplomatic community as a statement hinting at a possible realignment of the alliance structure.

## Scenarios Going Forward

If the Iran conflict transitions to a negotiation phase within <mark>April–June</mark>, there is a possibility that a Trump-Xi summit could be realized before summer. However, if the conflict drags on, the US-China negotiation vacuum could extend into <mark>the second half of 2026</mark>. This directly impacts the pace of global supply chain realignment and corporate investment decision-making.

<span class="hl-blue">Short-term beneficiaries</span>: Southeast Asian manufacturing hubs (Vietnam, Thailand, India) that stand to gain from continued US-China tensions. <span class="hl-red">Short-term losers</span>: Global manufacturers that have delayed supply chain restructuring in anticipation of improved US-China relations.
