The energy transition was supposed to reduce the world's dependence on politically volatile resources. Instead, it has created a new version of the same problem: a global race for <span class="hl-red">critical minerals</span> — rare earths, lithium, cobalt, nickel — that underpins everything from electric vehicle batteries to wind turbines and AI semiconductor manufacturing, concentrated in a handful of countries with complex geopolitical alignments.

## Key Takeaways

- Vietnam holds an estimated <mark>22 million tons</mark> of rare earth reserves — second only to China globally — and is emerging as a critical battleground between U.S. and Chinese strategic mineral interests
- China is exercising strategic restraint on both the Iran conflict and Venezuela, per Geopolitical Monitor analysis — a calculated long game that preserves resource relationships while letting adversaries overextend
- The Iran conflict creates a direct paradox: it intensifies the urgency of the energy transition while simultaneously disrupting the supply chains — shipping routes, energy costs, chemical inputs — needed to execute it

## The New Resource War: It's Not About Oil Anymore

The great geopolitical competition of the 21st century will not primarily be about oil. It will be about the materials required to replace oil. **Rare earth elements** — neodymium, dysprosium, terbium — are essential for the permanent magnets used in EV motors and wind turbines. **Lithium, cobalt, and nickel** form the core of battery chemistries. **Silicon and germanium** underpin semiconductor manufacturing that powers AI infrastructure.

China currently controls an estimated <mark>60%</mark> of global rare earth mining and approximately <mark>85%</mark> of rare earth processing capacity. This concentration creates precisely the kind of single-point-of-failure vulnerability in global supply chains that the U.S., EU, and their allies have warned about for years — and are now scrambling to address with varying degrees of urgency.

Benchmark Mineral Intelligence's keynote this week — "Rare Earths for Net Zero: Insulating the Green Transition from Geopolitical Shocks" — framed the core challenge: how do you build energy security when the materials required for energy security are themselves subject to geopolitical risk?

## Vietnam: The Unexpected Critical Minerals Node

The Diplomat's analysis this week highlighted **Vietnam** as an increasingly important player in the geopolitics of critical minerals. Vietnam holds significant rare earth deposits estimated at <mark>22 million tons</mark> — making it the world's second-largest reserves holder after China — and sits at the geographic intersection of competing U.S. and Chinese strategic interests in Southeast Asia.

Vietnam's rare earth advantage is not yet fully realized. The country lacks the processing infrastructure to convert raw mineral extraction into refined materials usable in high-tech manufacturing. This gap creates both an opportunity and a strategic choice:

- <span class="hl-blue">For the U.S. and allies</span>: Partnering with Vietnam on rare earth processing infrastructure could diversify the supply chain away from Chinese dominance at the processing stage — the most strategically sensitive bottleneck
- <span class="hl-red">For Vietnam</span>: Navigating between U.S. and Chinese strategic interests without becoming fully dependent on either requires extraordinary diplomatic agility — a balancing act that could define Vietnamese foreign policy for a generation
- <span class="hl-red">Environmental risk</span>: Rare earth mining generates significant environmental damage. Vietnam must balance development imperatives against sustainability commitments and domestic political sustainability

The U.S. has been actively pursuing mineral partnership agreements with resource-rich nations — Australia, Canada, the Philippines, and now Vietnam are in various stages of engagement. The goal: replicate the strategic diversification logic of the CHIPS Act, but for the physical materials layer of the energy transition.

## China's Calculated Restraint

Geopolitical Monitor published a nuanced analysis this week examining why China has remained largely passive on both the Iran conflict and the situation in Venezuela — despite having significant economic interests in both countries.

The answer, the analysis argues, is **strategic patience**. China has watched the U.S. expend diplomatic capital, military resources, and political attention on the Iran conflict while global oil prices surge toward <mark>$100</mark>. <span class="hl-blue">Higher oil prices hurt China's energy-intensive manufacturing sector in the short term, but they also weaken the U.S. economy, reduce Federal Reserve flexibility, and strain America's political credibility abroad.</span>

China appears willing to absorb short-term economic pain — higher input costs for its factories — in exchange for long-term strategic advantage. Meanwhile, restraint on Venezuela preserves China's relationships with Latin American resource-rich countries without the diplomatic costs of overt support.

This is a classic **"let the adversary overextend"** strategy, with deep roots in Chinese strategic tradition. The longer the U.S. is occupied with the Iran conflict, the less bandwidth it has to counter Chinese influence in Southeast Asia, Africa, and Latin America — precisely the regions where the critical minerals competition is being decided.

## The Green Transition Paradox

Here's the uncomfortable contradiction at the heart of current geopolitics: the shocks that make oil prices volatile are the same shocks that disrupt the supply chains needed to transition away from oil.

| Geopolitical Shock | Impact on Oil Markets | Impact on Green Transition Supply Chain |
|-------------------|-----------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Iran conflict | Price spike toward `$100+` | Shipping cost increases for mineral inputs |
| U.S.-China tensions | Export control escalation risk | Processing capacity concentrated in China at risk |
| Trade war tariffs | Supply chain restructuring costs | Critical mineral export restriction risk |
| Dollar strength | Oil priced higher for importers | Raises cost of importing mineral processing equipment |

The solution — building resilient, geographically diversified supply chains for critical minerals — requires exactly the kind of stable, long-horizon investment environment that geopolitical instability makes difficult. <span class="hl-red">This is the fundamental contradiction</span> that no energy transition roadmap has fully resolved.

## What Comes Next

Countries and companies that secure <span class="hl-blue">reliable, diversified access to critical minerals</span> in the next decade will hold structural advantages in everything from EV manufacturing to defense electronics to AI hardware. Those that don't will face the same vulnerabilities that oil dependency created in the 20th century — just expressed through battery supply chains instead of petroleum pipelines.

The U.S. CHIPS Act, the EU's Critical Raw Materials Act, and bilateral mineral partnership agreements represent early moves in what will be a multi-decade competition. <span class="hl-red">The risk is that policy responses are moving too slowly</span> relative to the speed at which geopolitical pressures are intensifying.

The decisions being made now — about where to mine, where to process, and who to partner with — will shape the geopolitical landscape of the 2030s. That makes Vietnam's rare earth reserves, China's strategic restraint, and the Iran conflict's supply chain disruptions all part of the same larger story: the contest over who controls the physical foundations of the next economy.
