High-level US-China dialogue is resuming, and analysts at South China Morning Post are now describing the trajectory as a potential <span class="hl-blue">tactical truce</span> — a deliberate, time-limited de-escalation between the world's two largest economies that could have outsized consequences for global supply chains, financial markets, and technology policy.

## Key Takeaways

- Trade negotiators from Washington and Beijing are described as "actively talking," with both sides signaling willingness to avoid further escalation
- A Trump visit to China is reported to be in planning stages, described as "likely limited to Beijing" — a constrained but symbolically significant diplomatic signal
- <span class="hl-red">Critical minerals</span> and <span class="hl-red">undersea cable infrastructure</span> have emerged as the new flashpoints beneath the broader trade dialogue

## Why "Tactical Truce" Rather Than Resolution

The terminology is precise. A "tactical truce" is not a trade deal, not a strategic framework, and not a normalization of relations. It is an agreement — explicit or implicit — to pause active escalation while both parties manage more pressing priorities.

For the United States in March 2026, the primary geopolitical priority has been the Iran conflict. For China, the more pressing concerns include managing domestic economic headwinds, maintaining export relationships in a fragile global demand environment, and avoiding the dual exposure of both US trade pressure and Middle East instability simultaneously.

The structural tensions between the two nations have not changed:

| Structural Flashpoint | Status |
|----------------------|--------|
| Technology export controls (AI chips, semiconductors) | Active |
| Critical minerals dominance | Active — US counter-strategy underway |
| Undersea cable infrastructure | Escalating (Chile cable dispute) |
| Taiwan status | Unchanged |
| TikTok / data sovereignty | Unresolved |

What has changed is the appetite for active escalation on both sides — at least temporarily. That is the definition of a tactical truce: neither side has resolved the underlying disputes, but both are choosing not to push them aggressively right now.

## The Critical Minerals Dimension

Beneath the trade dialogue, the United States is accelerating a counter-strategy to China's dominance in critical minerals — the rare earth elements and specialty metals that underpin everything from semiconductor fabrication to EV batteries to advanced military systems.

China currently controls processing capacity for <mark>over 85%</mark> of the world's rare earth elements. This structural advantage has given Beijing significant leverage in any trade confrontation — the threat of cutting critical mineral exports represents a supply chain disruption that would take years to work around.

The US is now pursuing an "innovative upgrades" strategy, according to South China Morning Post, combining domestic production incentives, allied-nation supply diversification (through Quad and Indo-Pacific Economic Framework partners), and recycling/substitution technologies. This is a multi-year project, not a near-term solution. But its existence changes the long-term leverage calculation.



## The Chile Cable Case: Infrastructure as Geopolitics

A less-discussed but increasingly significant flashpoint is undersea cable infrastructure. A submarine cable project in Chile has sparked a direct US-China power struggle — with Washington pushing to exclude Chinese telecommunications companies from the project and Beijing asserting its right to compete for infrastructure contracts in Latin America.

This is not a bilateral US-China issue. It is a template for dozens of similar disputes across the Global South, where Chinese state-backed infrastructure investment and US national security concerns are in direct competition. The outcome of the Chile case will set precedents for how other countries navigate the same choice.

<span class="hl-red">Countries caught in the middle</span> face an increasingly binary choice: infrastructure with faster deployment and lower cost (China) or infrastructure that doesn't create future technology dependency concerns (US-aligned alternatives). Neither option is obviously superior, and the political and economic calculus varies by country.

## Trump's China Visit: Symbol vs. Substance

Reports that Trump's China trip will be "limited to Beijing" are significant in what they exclude as much as what they include. A visit to Beijing is a powerful symbolic normalization signal. Excluding other Chinese cities keeps the scope of diplomatic engagement contained — this is a handshake meeting, not a summit with joint communiqués and binding commitments.

> "High-level US-China dialogue is good for global stability." — South China Morning Post editorial

The editorial characterization is accurate precisely because it is minimal: stability is the achievable goal, not resolution. If the visit produces an agreement to continue talking and avoid near-term escalation on trade, it achieves its purpose.

## Market and Supply Chain Implications

A sustained tactical truce — even without structural resolution — would have meaningful consequences for global markets:

- <span class="hl-blue">Technology sector</span>: Reduced risk of additional semiconductor export escalation in the near term
- <span class="hl-blue">Consumer goods and retail</span>: Supply chain planning becomes more predictable as tariff uncertainty moderates
- <span class="hl-red">Critical minerals investors</span>: A truce reduces the urgency of diversification — Western ex-China supply chains may see slower investment inflows
- <span class="hl-blue">Shipping and logistics</span>: Normalization of trade volumes as both sides pull back from retaliatory posturing

The historical pattern with US-China tactical truces — the 2019 G20 truce being the most recent comparable — is that they buy time but do not resolve structural tensions. Markets typically price in the near-term relief but discount the medium-term uncertainty. That asymmetry is worth remembering.

## Outlook

The trajectory suggests a period of deliberate de-escalation that could last through the remainder of 2026 — driven by Iran-related mutual interest in global stability, domestic economic pressures on both sides, and the electoral calendar that makes sustained trade conflict costly for both governments. The structural disputes over technology, critical minerals, and Taiwan will not be resolved in this window. But a tactical truce is not nothing: it buys space for supply chains to stabilize, for investment decisions to be made with slightly more confidence, and for the next round of structural negotiations to begin from a less adversarial baseline.
