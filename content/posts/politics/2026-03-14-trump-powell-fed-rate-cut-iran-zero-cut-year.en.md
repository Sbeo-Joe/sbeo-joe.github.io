A federal judge dismissed all subpoenas targeting **Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell** this week, citing what he called "essentially zero evidence" — even as President Trump publicly demanded rate cuts "IMMEDIATELY," a pressure campaign that analysts say could undermine the very market stability Trump claims to want.

## Key Takeaways

- A federal judge blocked all subpoenas in the Powell investigation using unusually emphatic language — a meaningful win for central bank institutional independence
- Trump's public pressure campaign continues despite the Iran conflict he launched being the primary reason rate cuts are now off the table in 2026
- Bloomberg reports traders are no longer fully pricing in even a single Fed rate cut this year — a dramatic reversal from expectations of `2–3 cuts` just weeks ago

## The Powell Subpoena: What the Court Decided

The legal battle over Federal Reserve independence reached a decisive moment this week. A federal judge blocked all subpoenas that had been issued as part of an investigation into Fed Chair **Jerome Powell**, writing that the effort presented "essentially zero evidence" to justify the intrusion. The language was unusually pointed — not merely finding insufficient grounds, but characterizing the evidentiary basis as essentially nonexistent.

The subpoenas had raised alarm among economists, former Fed officials, and international financial institutions. The Fed's **political independence** — its ability to set interest rate policy based on economic data rather than political pressure — is considered a cornerstone of global financial market stability. Any credible threat to that independence tends to weaken confidence in the U.S. dollar and Treasury markets.

The judge's ruling is a meaningful institutional signal: the courts, at least for now, are willing to protect the central bank from what critics characterized as politically motivated legal harassment.

## Trump's Rate-Cut Campaign: Loud, Public, and Self-Defeating

Even as the subpoenas were being blocked, President Trump continued his public pressure campaign. Via social media and press remarks, Trump demanded Powell cut rates "IMMEDIATELY," describing the current interest rate environment as damaging to U.S. economic competitiveness and growth.

The irony is stark and widely noted: <span class="hl-red">Trump's own Iran conflict is the single biggest reason rates cannot be cut right now.</span> With oil above <mark>$100 per barrel</mark> due to the Middle East war, inflation is re-accelerating. Cutting rates into rising inflation would be economically reckless — and Powell and the FOMC know it.

> "Trump demands Fed cut rates. His Iran war has investors betting otherwise."

As Reuters framed it directly, the market has absorbed both sides of the contradiction and made its judgment. Rates are staying higher for longer because of the energy-driven inflation surge — a surge that Trump's own policy decisions have materially amplified. Fortune went further, noting that a **zero-cut year** is looking increasingly likely.

## The Rate-Cut Forecast Collapse

At the start of 2026, Wall Street consensus forecast two to three Federal Reserve rate cuts by year-end. That consensus has now collapsed entirely:

| Period | Market-Implied Rate Cuts for 2026 |
|--------|----------------------------------|
| January 2026 | `2–3 cuts` expected |
| February 2026 | `1–2 cuts` expected |
| March 14, 2026 | `0 cuts` fully priced in |

Bloomberg's report that traders are no longer fully pricing in even one cut is a major shift. The Fed funds futures market — the most direct gauge of market rate expectations — now reflects a base case of rates staying unchanged through year-end. For **mortgage borrowers**, this is concrete bad news. Yahoo Finance noted that Middle East war "clouds the outlook" on when mortgage rates might eventually fall, and the answer, for now, is: not this year.

## Why Fed Independence Matters Beyond the Headlines

Central bank independence is not merely an academic principle — it has direct, measurable consequences for everyday economic conditions. When markets believe a central bank may be politically pressured into cutting rates prematurely, **inflation expectations rise** even before the cuts happen. Rising inflation expectations make actual inflation harder to control, creating a self-reinforcing feedback loop.

The judge's decision blocking the Powell subpoenas is therefore important not just as a legal matter but as a <span class="hl-blue">global market confidence signal</span>. Fed independence — even if only partially preserved through legal challenge — supports dollar strength, Treasury market stability, and the international credibility of U.S. monetary policy. All three matter enormously during periods of geopolitical stress like the current Iran conflict.

International investors — sovereign wealth funds, foreign central banks, pension funds — hold <mark>trillions of dollars</mark> in U.S. Treasury securities partly because they trust the Fed to manage inflation independently of political cycles. Any sustained erosion of that trust would raise U.S. borrowing costs in ways that dwarf any short-term stimulus from premature rate cuts.

## Three Scenarios for Fed Policy in 2026

The path forward for monetary policy depends heavily on geopolitics:

**Scenario 1 — Iran de-escalation** (oil drops, inflation cools): Rate cuts return to the table in Q3 or Q4 2026. Markets would likely rally sharply on the first credible signal of this outcome.

**Scenario 2 — Status quo** (oil stays near $100, inflation elevated): Zero cuts in 2026, with the first cut potentially pushed to early 2027. This is now the base case per Bloomberg's reporting.

**Scenario 3 — Escalation** (Iran conflict widens, oil hits $120+): Emergency Fed assessment of financial stability risks. Rate cuts become impossible; market stress deepens.

<span class="hl-red">Scenario 2 is the market's base case.</span> For investors, this means duration risk in bond portfolios remains elevated, equity multiple compression continues, and the mortgage market stays frozen for most first-time buyers. The political pressure on the Fed will not stop — but for now, the courts have drawn a line. Whether that line holds is a question worth watching closely.
