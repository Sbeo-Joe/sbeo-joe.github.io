President Trump has shifted strategy toward offloading responsibility for defending the Strait of Hormuz onto allied nations. By <span class="hl-red">directly demanding troop deployments from South Korea, China, and Japan</span>, Trump has placed Seoul at the intersection of an energy emergency and a diplomatic dilemma.

## Key Takeaways

- Trump declares "countries that use the Strait of Hormuz should defend it themselves" — directly pressuring South Korea, China, and Japan for deployments
- South Korean government convenes emergency meeting to review evacuation options for Korean vessels in the Strait of Hormuz
- South Korea secures emergency crude supply of <mark>24 million barrels</mark> from the UAE — introduction of Russian crude also under consideration

## Trump's Strategic Pivot: From Requesting Support to Coercion

In the early stages of the Iran war, Trump sought voluntary support from allies. However, as Iranian forces mounted stronger-than-expected resistance and oil prices approached <mark>$110</mark>, the strategy shifted. Trump is now pressing South Korea, China, and Japan for direct troop deployments under the logic that "countries importing oil through the Strait of Hormuz should bear the cost of defending it."

![Image 1](https://assets.korearisk.com/uploads/sites/6/2026/03/Featured-Image-Empty-5-500x256.png)
*▲ South Korean government response regarding the defense of the Strait of Hormuz (Source: [Trump rejects allied support in Hormuz as pressure shifts to Seoul](https://koreapro.org/2026/03/trump-rejects-allied-support-in-hormuz-as-pressure-shifts-to-seoul/))*

While this logic appears superficially reasonable, it carries complex diplomatic implications. South Korea sources a significant portion of its crude oil imports through the Strait of Hormuz. So does China. Analysts assess that Trump's intent is to distribute America's military burden while simultaneously drawing South Korea, China, and Japan into direct involvement in the Iran war — thereby constraining their diplomatic options.

## South Korea's Dilemma and Emergency Response

The South Korean government is walking a difficult diplomatic tightrope, neither publicly refusing nor accepting the demand.

| Option | Advantages | Risks |
|--------|------------|-------|
| Accept deployment | Maintain credibility of U.S. alliance | Diplomatic conflict with Iran, domestic backlash |
| Reject deployment | Favorable domestic opinion, preserve Iran relations | Risk of Trump's trade and security pressure |
| Non-combat support | Middle ground | Potential U.S. dissatisfaction |

![Image 2](https://img.yna.co.kr/etc/inner/EN/2026/03/18/AEN20260318011151315_01_i_P2.jpg)
*▲ Government meeting on potential evacuation of South Korean vessels in the Strait of Hormuz (Source: [(LEAD) Gov't holds meeting on potential evacuation of S. Korean vessels in Hormuz Strait](https://en.yna.co.kr/view/AEN20260318011151315))*

On the energy front, South Korea has already moved swiftly. An aide to **President Lee Jae-myung** announced that <mark>24 million barrels</mark> of crude had been secured from the UAE on an emergency basis — a stockpile in preparation for a Hormuz blockade scenario. At the same time, Seoul is reportedly weighing the option of importing Russian crude, a move that could clash with Western sanctions against Russia and become yet another source of tension with Washington.

> "Whether Seoul agrees or refuses to deploy, Trump has already made South Korea the focal point of his strategic pressure."

## China's Calculus and Regional Dynamics

China has publicly rejected Trump's deployment demand as "interference in internal affairs." Ironically, the situation places the heaviest burden on South Korea's diplomatic strategy of maintaining balance between the U.S.-ROK alliance and its relationship with China. With the <span class="hl-red">Trump-Xi summit already postponed</span>, a scenario could unfold in which both Washington and Beijing simultaneously make conflicting demands of Seoul.

This situation — in which South Korea's energy security and alliance diplomacy are simultaneously put to the test — represents structural pressure that will persist until the Iran war concludes. How Seoul formally positions itself over the coming weeks is expected to mark a watershed moment in South Korean foreign policy.
