China has officially declared 2026 a <mark>"Landmark Year"</mark> for U.S.-China relations. The move is a clear precursor to arranging a summit between Trump and Xi Jinping, but the Iran war is adding a layer of complexity to an already difficult equation.

## Key Takeaways

- China's Ministry of Foreign Affairs and government authorities have officially designated 2026 as a "Landmark Year" for U.S.-China relations — signaling willingness to pursue a Trump visit to China or a Trump-Xi summit
- China's foreign minister stated the Iran war "should never have happened" — exploring the possibility of diplomatic alignment with the U.S.
- <span class="hl-red">The trade war, semiconductor restrictions, and the Taiwan issue</span> remain three formidable barriers to reconciliation

## China's Signal — Why Now

China has structural reasons for extending an olive branch to the United States. First, the Chinese economy is under significant slowdown pressure. A real estate market slump, weakening consumer sentiment, and declining exports are all hitting simultaneously, and the deteriorating relationship with the U.S. is accelerating these trends. Second, Beijing has recognized that the second Trump administration favors transactional diplomacy over a purely hardline approach. If China can put something on the negotiating table, there is room for improving relations.

According to AP reporting, China has designated 2026 as the inaugural year for restoring bilateral ties and is actively pursuing either a Trump visit to Beijing or a summit at a neutral location. Diplomatic circles in Beijing suggest that "if a Trump-Xi summit materializes, discussions on tariff reductions and easing semiconductor restrictions could begin."

## The Iran War Variable

However, the Iran war is complicating the equation. China's foreign minister stated that "the Iran war should never have happened." While this message signals that China — a partner of Iran — opposes the war itself, it can also be read as a diplomatic gesture aimed at creating space for cooperation with the United States.

From Washington's perspective, Chinese cooperation is needed while conducting the Iran war. China holds significant economic leverage over Iran, and simply not facilitating sanctions evasion would aid the U.S. war effort. This mutual interest could temporarily create common ground between the two powers.

| Issue | U.S. Position | China's Position |
|-------|--------------|------------------|
| Iran War | Seeking alliance and cooperation | Opposed but avoiding direct involvement |
| Trade Tariffs | Maintaining as a bargaining chip | Demanding swift reduction |
| Semiconductor Restrictions | Blocking China's tech advancement | Demanding complete removal |
| Taiwan | Maintaining the status quo | Emphasizing sovereignty |

## The U.S. Stance — A Complex Calculus

The Trump administration is employing a dual strategy of maintaining a tough stance on China while keeping the door open for deals. According to NBC News, the U.S. has clearly distanced itself from China's proposed framework of "two great powers co-managing the world." However, there are no signs that Washington would outright reject a summit.

> "The U.S. and China co-managing the world? That's China's vision, not America's." — NBC News analysis

## Outlook

If a U.S.-China summit actually materializes, it would send a positive signal to financial markets in the short term. <span class="hl-blue">Beneficiaries</span> would likely include companies with high bilateral trade exposure and semiconductor and equipment firms that could gain from eased tech export restrictions. On the other hand, <span class="hl-red">the trajectory of the Iran war</span> remains the biggest wildcard for bilateral diplomacy. If the war drags on and China clashes with the U.S. over Iran, the window for a summit could close rapidly. <mark>The first half of 2026</mark> appears to be the critical period that will determine whether this diplomatic restoration attempt succeeds or fails.
