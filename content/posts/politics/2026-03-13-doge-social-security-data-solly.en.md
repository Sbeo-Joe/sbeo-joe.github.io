A whistleblower has accused a <mark>Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE)</mark> operative of storing sensitive personal data from the Social Security Administration on a personal thumb drive — then taking that drive to a new job at a major defense contractor.

## Key Takeaways

- Whistleblower complaint alleges **John Solly** copied SSA data — potentially covering millions of Americans' Social Security numbers, income, and medical records — onto a personal storage device
- Solly subsequently took a position at **Leidos**, a <mark>$15B</mark> defense and government IT contractor with extensive intelligence and civilian agency contracts
- Both Solly and Leidos have issued strong denials; the case is before the SSA Office of Inspector General and has triggered congressional oversight inquiries

## Who Is John Solly?

John Solly is among the young, technically skilled operatives placed by DOGE into federal agencies over the past year as part of the administration's government efficiency mandate. These placements gave technologists direct access to agency IT systems and databases with less formal vetting than traditional federal hiring processes require.

The Social Security Administration holds some of the most sensitive personal data of any US government institution — records on <span class="hl-red">over 70 million Americans</span> including:

- Social Security numbers and full legal names
- Lifetime earnings and employment history
- Medical records for disability benefit claimants
- Bank account information for direct deposit recipients

A database combining these elements represents a near-comprehensive identity compromise toolkit for the American population — the type of dataset that nation-state adversaries and criminal organizations actively target.

## The Whistleblower Allegation

According to the complaint filed with the SSA's Office of Inspector General, Solly was overheard stating he had transferred SSA data onto a personal storage device and intended to bring it when he left for his new position. The complaint identifies **Leidos** as the destination employer.

The alleged conduct, if accurate, would represent a serious violation of federal data handling requirements under the **Federal Information Security Management Act (FISMA)**. Government regulations strictly prohibit transfer of sensitive personally identifiable information (PII) to unauthorized external devices or environments.

> "Removing government records from secure federal systems onto personal storage, then taking that storage to a private employer, would represent one of the most significant federal data breaches in recent memory — not through a cyberattack, but through deliberate human action."

**Leidos** is a publicly traded company with roughly <mark>$15B</mark> in annual revenue from US government contracts spanning defense, intelligence, and civilian agencies. The question of whether a Leidos employee possessed a database of Social Security records — and whether company leadership knew — carries significant legal and reputational implications.

Both Solly and Leidos have issued strong denials. Without an independent forensic investigation, the allegations remain contested.

## The Broader DOGE Data Access Problem

The Solly case crystallizes a systemic concern about DOGE's operational model. DOGE operatives were placed into federal agencies with the explicit goal of speed — moving faster than traditional civil service hiring and clearance processes permit. The efficiency rationale was genuine. <span class="hl-red">The trade-off was predictable:</span> reduced oversight of individuals with access to highly sensitive data.

This is not the first data access controversy involving DOGE-affiliated activities:

| Agency | Data Access Issue | Status |
|--------|------------------|--------|
| Treasury | Payment processing system access | Litigation ongoing |
| SSA | Alleged thumb drive data removal | Inspector General investigation |
| HHS | Database access claims | Congressional review |
| CFPB | Internal database access | Litigation |

## What Comes Next

The SSA Inspector General investigation is the primary active proceeding. If evidence supports the allegation, criminal referrals to the Department of Justice are possible under statutes covering unauthorized removal of government records and unauthorized computer access.

Multiple congressional oversight committees have sought briefings on DOGE's data practices across agencies. The Solly case provides the most specific allegation to date — a named individual, a specific alleged act, an identified destination employer — increasing the probability of formal hearings.

For the American public, the case raises a question that extends beyond one individual: when the people reviewing government systems operate outside normal accountability structures, who is responsible when something goes wrong? The institutions' response to this case will set the precedent for how government data protection is enforced in an era when the boundary between government and private sector has grown unusually porous.
