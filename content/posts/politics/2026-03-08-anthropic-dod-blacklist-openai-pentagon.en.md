<span class="hl-red">The U.S. Department of Defense</span> has officially designated Anthropic as a <span class="hl-red">supply chain risk</span>. Defense contractors and vendors must now submit certifications stating they do not use Anthropic models, effectively excluding Claude AI from the defense ecosystem. On the same day, <span class="hl-blue">OpenAI signed a contract with the Pentagon</span>.

## Key Takeaways

- Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth formally declared Anthropic a supply chain risk — Iran's use of Claude was the catalyst
- Defense contractors are now required to certify non-use of Anthropic models — Claude is effectively shut out of the defense market
- OpenAI signed a Pentagon contract on the very same day — the AI power race takes a geopolitical turn

## ⚖️ Why Anthropic? — Iran's Use of Claude

The immediate trigger for this designation was the discovery that Iran had been leveraging Anthropic's Claude. According to CNBC, the Department of Defense concluded that Anthropic had failed to adequately prevent adversarial nations from accessing its AI. A supply chain risk designation is far more than a warning. Once granted, defense and government contractors are required to submit formal certifications confirming they do not use that company's technology — a de facto market exclusion.

Anthropic is a company that has built its identity around AI safety and ethics. Founded by former OpenAI researchers who set out to prioritize responsible AI development, it is a profound blow that this very company has been classified — paradoxically — as an unsafe supply chain risk. For Anthropic, geopolitical judgment rather than technical controls has become the determining factor in the company's fate.

> "Defense vendors must certify that they are not using Anthropic models." — CNBC

## 🤝 OpenAI's Strategic Windfall

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman wasted no time weighing in on Anthropic's situation. Altman emphasized the principle that governments must be more powerful than corporations, and argued that OpenAI operates in accordance with that principle. The remarks came immediately after Anthropic was added to the blacklist.

<span class="hl-blue">OpenAI's Pentagon contract</span> completes this picture. Two of the biggest players in the AI industry are now on entirely divergent paths when it comes to their relationship with the U.S. military and national security apparatus.

| | Anthropic | OpenAI |
|---|---|---|
| DoD Status | <span class="hl-red">Supply Chain Risk</span> | <span class="hl-blue">Contract Partner</span> |
| Government Market | Effectively excluded | Successfully entered |
| CEO Stance | Responding to fallout | Championing government primacy |

## 🌐 The Geopoliticization of AI Governance

This episode makes clear that the future of AI companies will not be determined by technical capability alone. We have entered an era where which nations and actors can access an AI model — and how effectively a company controls that access — is directly tied to corporate survival.

The race for AI dominance in defense and intelligence has now spilled into competition among companies within the United States itself. <span class="hl-blue">OpenAI</span> has chosen a strategy of leveraging government trust to capture the security market early, while <span class="hl-red">Anthropic</span> finds itself on the losing end of that contest. How Google, Meta, Microsoft, and other tech giants read and respond to this new landscape will be the next storyline to watch.
