The Trump administration launched new trade investigations targeting key US partners this week — signaling the next escalation phase of a protectionist agenda that has already reshaped global supply chains. Meanwhile, the <mark>$100,000 H-1B visa fee</mark> imposed last fall is creating cascading real-world chaos that its architects apparently did not anticipate.

## Key Takeaways

- Trump launched new Section 301-style trade investigations targeting the EU, India, Canada, and Mexico — the procedural step that leads to formal tariff findings within <mark>6–12 months</mark>
- The <mark>$100,000</mark> H-1B fee has stranded workers abroad at consulates, created teacher shortages in international schools, and left hospitals with staffing gaps
- Both policies follow the same pattern: <span class="hl-red">maximalist opening positions that impose large near-term costs in exchange for uncertain long-term leverage</span>

## The New Trade War Phase: From Tariffs to Investigations

The Trump administration's first-term and current-term tariff regimes — broad levies on Chinese goods, steel and aluminum tariffs on allies, USMCA renegotiation pressure — represented phase one. Investigations are phase two.

Section 301 investigations, authorized by the Trade Act of 1974, allow the USTR to investigate "unreasonable or discriminatory" trade practices and impose retaliatory duties. They are the legal mechanism through which the original China tariffs were imposed.

Reported investigation targets include:

| Country/Bloc | Alleged Practice |
|-------------|-----------------|
| European Union | Digital services taxes targeting US tech companies |
| India | Pharmaceutical and agricultural market restrictions |
| Canada | Dairy market protections and digital taxes |
| Mexico | Near-shoring benefits not passing to US workers |

Investigations do not produce immediate tariffs — they are a <mark>6–12 month</mark> process creating leverage for negotiation. The message to trading partners: come to the table now, or face formal findings justifying new duties.

> "Trump launches the next phase of his trade wars with new investigations of key partners."

## What Changes: Supply Chain and Corporate Strategy

For multinationals, the announcement triggers familiar scenario planning. Companies that near-shored manufacturing to Mexico to avoid China tariffs now face the possibility that Mexican operations become the next target.

<span class="hl-blue">Near-term winners</span>: domestic US manufacturers in targeted sectors; trade compliance legal and consulting firms; companies with fully US-based supply chains.

<span class="hl-red">Near-term losers</span>: exporters to the US from EU, India, Canada, Mexico; US companies with production in those countries; consumers facing price increases.

The investigations also complicate coalition-building for the Strait of Hormuz response. Trump has asked allies including the UK, France, Japan, and South Korea to send warships to protect oil shipping lanes — while simultaneously opening trade investigations against some of those same allies.

## The H-1B $100,000 Fee: Chaos in Practice

The H-1B visa fee increase — from approximately <mark>$5,000</mark> to <mark>$100,000</mark> per petition — was signed as an executive order in fall 2025. The stated rationale: raise the cost of hiring foreign skilled labor, making US workers more competitive.

The actual outcome has been significantly more chaotic. The fee structure created a trap for workers already in the US on H-1B status who needed to travel abroad to renew their visa stamps — a routine requirement that historically involved a consulate visit to India, Canada, or Mexico. When they arrived, they discovered:

- Processing times extended dramatically as consulates sought guidance
- Workers were stranded abroad for weeks or months, unable to return
- Employers faced uncertainty about whether workers could legally return

The Verge's reporting identified additional unintended consequences: teacher shortages at schools employing H-1B teachers, disruptions to healthcare staffing, and legal uncertainty for tech companies with thousands of H-1B holders.

| Sector | H-1B Dependency | Impact of $100K Fee |
|--------|----------------|---------------------|
| Big Tech | High | Cost increase, talent pipeline disrupted |
| Hospitals/Healthcare | High | <span class="hl-red">Physician and nurse shortages</span> |
| Education | Medium | <span class="hl-red">Teacher program disruptions</span> |
| Research universities | High | <span class="hl-red">Faculty hiring freezes</span> |

## The Policy Logic — and Its Gaps

Both policies follow the same logic: impose costs on foreign inputs to shift competitive advantage to domestic alternatives. The theory is coherent. The execution reveals its weaknesses.

H-1B restrictions shift advantage to domestic workers only if domestic workers exist in sufficient numbers to fill the roles. In healthcare, engineering, and specialized research, that domestic supply does not currently exist. <span class="hl-red">The gap between policy intent and outcome</span> is widest precisely in sectors most dependent on H-1B workers.

The fee is already in litigation, with multiple challenges to its legal basis moving through courts. Whether the trade investigations produce negotiated outcomes or trigger retaliatory measures that raise costs on US exporters will be the key test of phase two.
