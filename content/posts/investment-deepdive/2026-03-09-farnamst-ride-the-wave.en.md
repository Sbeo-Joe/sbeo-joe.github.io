Kodak once employed <mark>140,000</mark> people and dominated global photography for generations. In <mark>1975</mark>, Kodak's own engineers invented the digital camera internally — and the company chose not to ride the wave, defending its film monopoly until it was too late. Today, Kodak serves as the canonical example of what Charlie Munger calls "competitive destruction," and Farnam Street's mental model of wave-riding may be the most important strategic framework for both investors and executives navigating today's technology landscape.

> 📝 **Original Article**: [Ride the Wave](https://fs.blog/ride-wave/) — Farnam Street

## Key Takeaways

- 🔑 Kodak invented the digital camera in <mark>1975</mark> but chose to protect its film business — the textbook case of missing a technological wave, leading directly to bankruptcy despite once employing 140,000 people
- 🔑 Munger's "surfing" mental model: early wave-catchers don't merely succeed — they often dominate entire industries for extended periods through compounding network effects, switching costs, and scale economies
- 🔑 <span class="hl-red">Missing the wave is not just costly — it can be existential</span>: buggy whip manufacturers, video rental stores, and taxi medallion owners were all destroyed by failing to identify and ride the next technological wave

## ⚡ Competitive Destruction: The Operating System of Modern Markets

Charlie Munger articulated the mechanism with characteristic precision in *Poor Charlie's Almanack*:

> "When technology moves as fast as it does in a civilization like ours, you get a phenomenon that I call competitive destruction. You know, you have the finest buggy whip factory, and all of a sudden, in comes this little horseless carriage. And before too many years go by, your buggy whip business is dead. You either get into a different business or you're dead — you're destroyed. It happens again and again and again."

This is not metaphor. It is the operating system of modern markets. Every decade produces new technological waves — and each wave destroys incumbents who cling to the previous paradigm while simultaneously creating extraordinary wealth for those who catch the new one early.

The historical pattern is remarkably consistent:

| Old Industry | Disrupting Wave | Key Losers |
|---|---|---|
| Buggy whips | Automobile | Entire horse-carriage ecosystem |
| Film photography | Digital cameras | Kodak (<mark>140,000</mark> employees → bankruptcy) |
| Video rental | Streaming services | Blockbuster, Movie Gallery |
| Taxi medallions | Ridesharing apps | Medallion owners (values collapsed from millions to near-zero) |

<span class="hl-red">In each case, the disrupted incumbents were not unintelligent or underfunded.</span> Kodak had brilliant engineers — they literally built the disrupting technology. Blockbuster had sophisticated logistics operations. Taxi companies had regulatory moats that seemed impenetrable for decades. What they lacked was the willingness — or organizational capacity — to abandon their existing advantage and paddle toward the approaching wave.

## 🏄 The Surfing Mental Model: Three Phases of Wave-Catching

Munger's surfing analogy carries precise strategic content: "When a surfer gets up and catches the wave and just stays there, he can go a long, long time. But if he gets off the wave, he becomes mired in shallows."

This model has three distinct phases, each with its own challenge and its own set of investor implications.

### Phase 1: Spotting the Wave Before It Crests

The highest-value skill in technology investing is identifying waves early — before mainstream consensus has priced in the potential. This requires distinguishing genuine structural shifts from temporary trends, a skill that is far harder than it sounds.

Most "next big things" are false alarms; the genuine technological waves are often initially dismissed by incumbents and analysts alike. <span class="hl-blue">Amazon was called a bookstore. Netflix was called a DVD company. Tesla was called a niche luxury brand.</span> In each case, wave-catchers with the right framework saw a *platform* being built, not a *product* being sold.

The diagnostic question is: does this innovation change the underlying *economics* of an industry permanently? If yes, it's a wave. If it merely improves existing economics without restructuring them, it's likely a ripple.

### Phase 2: Paddling Toward It (The Courage Phase)

Catching a wave before it becomes obvious requires courage. <span class="hl-red">Early positioning always feels uncomfortable</span> — valuations seem stretched relative to near-term fundamentals, the business model isn't yet proven at scale, and conventional wisdom argues for patience or caution.

This is precisely when future dominators are accumulating position, often against fierce skepticism. The investors who caught NVIDIA's AI infrastructure wave in `2022–2023`, when the company was widely perceived as primarily a gaming chip maker, exemplify this phase. The wave was visible — the explosive GPU demand driven by large language model training — but the courage to size the position appropriately and hold through the drawdowns was not common.

The psychological challenge of this phase cannot be understated. Many investors spot the wave correctly but fail to paddle hard enough — taking small, hedge-size positions that acknowledge the opportunity without capturing its full magnitude.

### Phase 3: Riding It (The Compounding Phase)

Once a wave is caught, the compounding dynamics are extraordinary and self-reinforcing. The early mover accumulates customers, data, talent, and capital at rates that late entrants simply cannot match. <span class="hl-blue">Network effects, switching costs, and scale economies create the moats that allow early wave-catchers to maintain dominance for years or decades.</span>

This is why the returns to early wave identification are so disproportionate. Being right about the direction of technological change is table stakes — the asymmetric returns come from being *early* and *staying long* through the compounding phase. The investors who sold Amazon at `$50` because the valuation seemed rich missed the compounding decade; those who sold NVIDIA at `$500` in 2023 made the same error.

## 📈 Investment Implications: Translating the Mental Model to Portfolio Management

The "ride the wave" framework has concrete implications for portfolio construction in `2025–2026`.

**Identify the current waves.** Several structural shifts are simultaneously cresting:

- <span class="hl-blue">Artificial intelligence / foundation model infrastructure</span>: the buildout of data centers, power generation, and cooling systems is a multi-year capital expenditure wave
- <span class="hl-blue">GLP-1 drugs and metabolic health</span>: Ozempic and its successors represent a potential restructuring of healthcare economics at the `$100B+` scale
- <span class="hl-blue">Defense technology and reshoring</span>: geopolitical fragmentation is driving sustained government procurement cycles
- <span class="hl-red">Legacy media and physical retail</span>: these waves are in late-stage decline; riding them means shorting the laggards, not buying the dips

**Respect the wave's duration.** Munger's insight that a surfer "can go a long, long time" if they stay on the wave is critically underappreciated by most investors. The psychological pressure to take profits early — or rotate into the "next" wave prematurely — is one of the most costly mistakes in growth investing. <span class="hl-red">Selling a position in a genuine wave-rider because it "feels expensive" has destroyed more cumulative return than almost any other behavioral error.</span>

**Watch for signs of falling off.** Every wave eventually crashes. The signals are usually competitive: new entrants with structural cost advantages, technological leapfrogging that makes the current leader's approach obsolete, or regulatory disruption that removes the moat. Actively monitoring these signals — rather than assuming permanent dominance — is what separates successful wave-riding from being wiped out by the next wave.

## 🔮 The Open Question: Which Waves to Catch Next

Farnam Street's framework is descriptive rather than predictive — it tells you *how* waves work, not *which* specific waves to catch. That analytical step remains the investor's core task and competitive edge.

The framework suggests concentrating analytical attention on industries where: technology is moving fastest, incumbent defensiveness (a sign of structural threat) is highest, and first-mover advantages compound most aggressively through network effects or data moats.

The investors who get this right — consistently, not just once — are the ones who build enduring wealth. The waves keep coming. The question is always whether you are paddling toward them early, or watching from the shore as others ride them to extraordinary heights.
