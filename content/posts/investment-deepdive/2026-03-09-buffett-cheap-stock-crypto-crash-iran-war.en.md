## Key Takeaways

- Iran war fears triggered double-digit percentage declines in Bitcoin and Ethereum; XRP and Dogecoin held up relatively better
- Motley Fool analysis: a Buffett portfolio holding has entered a historically cheap valuation zone — "absurdly inexpensive"
- Crisis markets reconfirm the defensive power of <span class="hl-blue">cash-flow-driven value stocks</span>

## Why Crypto Is More Vulnerable in a Crisis

In the immediate aftermath of the Iran war outbreak, Bitcoin posted double-digit losses while XRP and Dogecoin suffered shallower drawdowns. This divergence signals that capital-rotation patterns within crypto are fragmenting. In risk-off regimes, investors tend to liquidate Bitcoin first — it carries the largest market cap and deepest liquidity.

The deeper reason for crypto's crisis fragility is the <span class="hl-red">absence of an intrinsic-value anchor</span>. Equities have earnings, assets, and cash flows as a valuation floor; crypto has no fundamental support to arrest panic selling when fear spikes. A triple headwind — crude oil surpassing <mark>$110</mark>, a global equity selloff, and a surging dollar — hit crypto squarely.

## Buffett's Holdings — Why They Hold Up

According to Motley Fool analysis, a specific stock held in Berkshire Hathaway's portfolio has entered a historically undervalued range. The stock meets all three of Buffett's core investment criteria: strong and durable free cash flow, low leverage, and a recession-resistant business model.

> "Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful." — Warren Buffett

When market fear peaks — as it does now — Buffett-style investors find themselves with the most attractive entry points. Berkshire Hathaway is reported to be sitting on more than <mark>$330 billion</mark> in cash, positioning it perfectly to deploy capital during market dislocations.

## Signals to Watch Now

High-valuation AI growth names like Palantir face a dual squeeze: rising rate risk and geopolitical fear compounding simultaneously. By contrast, Berkshire-affiliated value holdings with strong earnings resilience become relatively more attractive as market fear intensifies.

Every crisis stress-tests a portfolio's true quality. The lesson this Iran war shock reinforces is clear: <span class="hl-blue">assets rooted in fundamental value</span> hold their anchor even in a fear-driven market. Now is the moment to audit your portfolio and revisit the Buffett playbook.
