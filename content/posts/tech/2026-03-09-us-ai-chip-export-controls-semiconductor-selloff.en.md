U.S. government AI semiconductor export controls are moving toward <span class="hl-red">sweeping expansion that includes allied nations</span>. Vicor shares plunged  in a single session as fear psychology spread across the semiconductor sector.

## Key Takeaways

- U.S. officially considering extending AI chip export controls to allied nations — mandatory U.S. investment requirements for foreign companies also reportedly included
- Vicor shares drop  on export control fears alone, selloff spreading across semiconductor sector
- South Korea's <mark>3.3 billion (approx. 18 trillion won)</mark> semiconductor trade surplus is directly exposed to this regulatory uncertainty

## What Changes

Existing U.S. AI chip export controls primarily targeted adversary nations such as China and Russia. The new regulatory proposal reported by TechCrunch and Reuters would apply **without exception to allied nations** as well, with a conditional model under consideration: foreign companies would only be permitted to purchase advanced AI chips if they make investments of a certain scale in the United States.

This is effectively interpreted as pressure on global companies seeking to access the U.S. semiconductor ecosystem to first establish factories and investments in the U.S. Chips critical to AI training and inference — such as Nvidia's H100/B200 series — are likely to be included among export-controlled items.

> "Export control expansion is not just trade policy — it is a core weapon in the global AI supremacy competition."

## Supply Chain Risk: Who Gets Hurt

South Korea, with a semiconductor trade surplus of <mark>3.3 billion</mark>, is far from exempt from this regulatory expansion. HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) produced by Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix is a critical component of Nvidia GPUs, and the entire supply chain could come within scope of the export control reshaping.

<span class="hl-red">Near-term damage:</span> Regulatory uncertainty may cause semiconductor companies to delay capital investment plans and reduce the likelihood of large advance orders from global customers.

<span class="hl-blue">Paradoxical beneficiaries:</span> Companies that made early U.S. investments — TSMC's Arizona fab, Samsung's Texas fab — have already secured regulatory bypass routes. If a conditional permit structure is introduced, these companies will be well-positioned.

## Structural Perspective

Another notable aspect of this regulatory proposal is the **mandatory U.S. investment requirement for foreign companies**. This is a structural change that goes beyond simple export restrictions, effectively compelling global semiconductor firms to deeply integrate into the U.S. supply chain. The U.S. is pursuing a strategy of linking AI chip supply chains to geopolitical alliance relationships.

## Outlook

U.S. AI chip export controls are evolving in an ever-tightening direction. If this proposal is finalized, global semiconductor companies will need to adapt to a new environment where U.S. market access requires mandatory U.S. investment. Both the semiconductor industry and investors need to closely monitor when the regulations are finalized and what specific conditions are imposed.
