The semiconductor super cycle has at least <mark>two more years to run</mark> — and China is betting that by <mark>2030</mark>, it will no longer need Western supply chains to sustain its technology ambitions. Meanwhile, <span class="hl-blue">IonQ and the University of Cambridge just announced a partnership to build the UK's most powerful quantum computer</span>.

## Key Takeaways

- New analysis forecasts the semiconductor super cycle extending through <mark>2028</mark>, driven by AI inference demand with no foreseeable ceiling
- China is accelerating toward full chip independence by <mark>2030</mark>, with CXMT and SMIC receiving record state investment as ASML alternatives develop domestically
- IonQ's partnership with Cambridge represents a major expansion of quantum computing infrastructure outside the US — the quantum race is globalizing

## The Super Cycle: Why AI Demand Has No Off Switch

Semiconductor cycles have historically followed a boom-bust pattern. The current cycle is structurally different. The demand driver is not consumer hardware — it is AI. And AI demand compounds: each model generation requires more compute, and inference is continuous and expanding.

NVIDIA's data center revenue grew from <mark>$3.6 billion</mark> in Q1 2023 to over <mark>$30 billion</mark> per quarter by late 2025. The TSMC-produced chips at the center of that demand remain constrained by both wafer capacity and advanced packaging at TSMC's CoWoS facilities.

| AI Chip Demand Driver | Why It Extends the Cycle |
|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Training (frontier models) | Models double in parameter count ~18 months |
| Inference (deployment) | Per-user compute grows with adoption |
| Sovereign AI infrastructure | Governments building national compute clusters |
| Edge AI | Advanced node demand expanding to mobile and auto |

## China's All-In Bid for Chip Independence

US export controls — banning ASML's extreme ultraviolet (EUV) machines and NVIDIA's most powerful chips — have pushed China to the largest state-directed semiconductor investment in history.

CXMT (ChangXin Memory Technologies) is now producing DRAM chips within two generations of Samsung and SK Hynix. SMIC has pushed production to <mark>7nm</mark> using older DUV lithography through multi-patterning techniques. The official target: <span class="hl-red">full technology independence by 2030</span>.

> "China's semiconductor strategy has shifted from catch-up to decoupling."

A China that no longer needs ASML, TSMC, or NVIDIA for its domestic AI programs is a China that faces trade restrictions with far less economic pain. The pressure on Western export control regimes increases as their effectiveness window narrows.

## Quantum Computing: The Cambridge Partnership

IonQ, the publicly traded US quantum computing company using trapped-ion technology, has announced a partnership with the University of Cambridge to build the UK's most powerful quantum computer.

The significance is not just the hardware — it is the geography. Bringing IonQ's commercial quantum infrastructure to Cambridge places quantum computing capacity in Europe's most important research ecosystem, creating a node outside the US-China bilateral competition.

| Quantum Approach | Key Players | Gate Fidelity |
|-----------------|------------|---------------|
| Trapped-ion | IonQ, Quantinuum | <mark>>99.9%</mark> |
| Superconducting | IBM, Google | ~99.5% |
| Photonic | PsiQuantum | Pre-commercial |

## The Intersection: Classical and Quantum Converge on AI

The semiconductor super cycle, China's independence drive, and the quantum buildout converge on the same destination: the future of AI compute. Classical silicon will carry AI inference for the foreseeable future. Quantum systems may transform AI training on a longer horizon. And China's independence drive will determine whether AI's compute infrastructure remains globally integrated or fragments into rival supply chains.

The <mark>2030</mark> horizon China has set is also roughly when quantum computers are projected to demonstrate meaningful advantage on real-world problems. Whether the two timelines intersect — and in whose favor — is the most consequential open question in technology today.
