On the same day the Pentagon formally labeled Anthropic a national security threat, Claude was already running in U.S. military systems supporting Iran war operations. This paradox marks a historic confrontation between Silicon Valley and the Department of Defense.

## Key Takeaways

- Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth officially designated Anthropic as a <mark>"National Security Supply Chain Risk"</mark> — defense contractors must now certify they don't use Claude in Pentagon work
- Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei: "We have no choice but to challenge this in court" — the first AI company to directly contest a DoD supply-chain designation
- OpenAI robotics chief Caitlin Kalinowski resigned immediately over the Pentagon deal — internal fractures now fully public

## The Core Contradiction: Designated and Deployed Simultaneously

CNBC confirmed the stunning irony: <span class="hl-red">at the very moment DoD declared Anthropic a threat, Claude was actively supporting Iran war operations</span> inside U.S. military systems. As a result of the designation, defense contractors must submit certifications to the Pentagon stating they will not use Anthropic models in their government work. AWS clarified that Claude remains available for non-defense commercial use — but the contradictions are glaring.

Why now? Analysts point to two scenarios. First, the Trump administration may be leveraging concerns about Anthropic's investor base (alleged ties to Chinese capital) to extend Pentagon control over AI firms. Second, with the Iran war intensifying, the administration may be using supply-chain designations to pressure AI companies into defense cooperation. Either way, the message is clear: <span class="hl-blue">AI companies must now choose sides.</span>

> "This is not a choice we can accept. We have no choice but to challenge this in court." — Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei

## Ripple Effects: Billions in Contracts on the Move

The immediate impact: <span class="hl-red">defense contractors may need to swap out Claude-based systems</span>, and OpenAI, Microsoft, and Palantir are well-positioned to fill the gap. Palantir already surged <mark>+15%</mark> in the week of the designation — a market signal of who benefits.

Longer term, AI companies will face mounting pressure to establish explicit ethical frameworks before pursuing military contracts. Google's 2018 Maven withdrawal created an informal industry norm. That norm is now being dismantled in real time.

The unresolved question that will define AI governance for years: if an AI company can be classified as a war asset without democratic oversight, how should the dual-use nature of AI be regulated? <span class="hl-blue">Anthropic's legal challenge could become the landmark case that determines the answer.</span>
