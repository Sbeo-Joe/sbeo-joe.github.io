OpenAI robotics hardware lead Caitlin Kalinowski resigned <span class="hl-red">immediately after the DoD contract signing</span>. With Anthropic's Pentagon controversy overlapping at the same time, the flames of the "AI militarization ethics" debate are reigniting across the entire AI industry.

## Key Takeaways

- OpenAI robotics hardware lead Kalinowski publicly announces resignation on X immediately after DoD contract
- Anthropic also facing fierce criticism from internal employees and external researchers over Pentagon collaboration
- <span class="hl-red">AI militarization dilemma</span> — federal funding vs. talent exodus vs. public trust

## Kalinowski's Resignation — "What I Built Cannot Become a Weapon"

According to Engadget, Kalinowski publicly announced her resignation on X (Twitter) with a direct reference to the DoD contract. She was a key figure who had led OpenAI's robotics hardware strategy and had been responsible for the physical embodiment of AI robots. It is being received by the industry as not a simple personnel change, but an <span class="hl-red">explicit expression of opposition to OpenAI's militarization direction</span>.

Kalinowski's departure is not simply the replacement of one executive. The strategic direction of the entire robotics hardware division could waver, and it will inevitably affect the morale and direction of the team she led. In the AI industry, top hardware engineers can choose where they want to work. The possibility of this resignation triggering a chain of departures within the team cannot be ruled out.

## The Anthropic Controversy — The Paradox of an "AI Safety" Company Working with the Pentagon

According to TechCrunch, Anthropic's contract with the DoD drew fierce backlash from internal employees and external researchers. Anthropic was founded with AI safety as its core value. The gap between the founding principle of "building safe AI" and military contracting is at the heart of the criticism.

> "We said we would build safe AI. Whose 'safety' are we talking about if that safe AI is used on the battlefield?"

TechCrunch analyzed that this controversy reveals not just Anthropic's problem but a structural dilemma facing all leading AI companies including OpenAI and Google DeepMind. Federal government contracts provide massive funding and infrastructure, but at the cost of key talent departures and erosion of public trust.

## Three Positions on AI Militarization

| Position | Argument | Representative Example |
|----------|----------|------------------------|
| Pro-collaboration | Defending democracies, responsible AI application | OpenAI CEO Sam Altman |
| Conditional collaboration | Exclude lethal autonomous weapons | Google (re-entry after 2019 Maven withdrawal) |
| Anti-collaboration | Violates AI principles, talent exodus | Kalinowski resignation, employee petitions |

## Talent Exodus Is the Real Risk

The core lesson of this situation is a **business risk** that goes beyond simple ethics debate. The <mark>2018 Google Project Maven</mark> scenario — where thousands signed petitions and some quit — could repeat. With AI talent competition at a fierce peak, internal defections are not a short-term loss but a medium-to-long-term competitive threat.

The so-called "Pro-Human Declaration" — a public pledge to limit AI development direction to civilian and humanitarian purposes — was announced just before the DoD-Anthropic controversy, reflecting the mood across the industry. Kalinowski's resignation could become a turning point in how AI companies set the balance between military contracts and technology ethics.
