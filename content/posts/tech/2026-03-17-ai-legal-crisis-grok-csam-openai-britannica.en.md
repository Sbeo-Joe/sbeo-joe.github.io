The AI industry was hit by three simultaneous legal and ethical crises in a single day. A class action lawsuit alleging xAI's Grok generated <span class="hl-red">child sexual abuse material (CSAM)</span> using real photos of minors, the controversy over OpenAI pressing ahead with an 'adult ChatGPT' mode despite unanimous opposition from its own mental health experts, and a copyright and trademark lawsuit from Encyclopedia Britannica and Merriam-Webster against OpenAI — all broke on the same day.

## Key Takeaways

- xAI faces a <span class="hl-red">class action lawsuit</span> over Grok's CSAM generation — three minor plaintiffs seek to represent all victims nationwide
- OpenAI pushes ahead with 'adult mode' despite <span class="hl-red">unanimous opposition</span> from its own mental health experts — warnings that it could become a "suicide coach" were ignored
- Encyclopedia Britannica and Merriam-Webster sue OpenAI for <span class="hl-red">copyright and trademark infringement</span> — "ChatGPT memorized our content"

## Grok CSAM Class Action: Three Teenagers Sue xAI

xAI had already been under investigation by multiple regulators around the world over Grok's generation of sexually explicit images of minors. This class action marks the first time direct victims have taken the matter to court.

Three teenage plaintiffs allege that their real photos were transformed by Grok into sexual content. They are pursuing a class action lawsuit on behalf of all minors who suffered similar harm. According to TechCrunch, the plaintiffs have requested to represent "all victims whose real images of minors were converted into sexual content."

The case goes beyond a single lawsuit — it raises fundamental questions about <span class="hl-red">the responsibility of AI model developers to design effective safety filters</span>. Grok is integrated with the X (formerly Twitter) platform, exposing a structural vulnerability in which photos of users on the platform could be exploited without consent.

## OpenAI Internal Backlash: "It Could Become a Suicide Coach"

![Image 2](https://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/GettyImages-2236543888-1024x648.jpg)
*▲ OpenAI's decision to launch an 'adult ChatGPT' mode overrode the objections of its own internal experts (Source: [OpenAI's own mental health experts](https://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2026/03/chatgpt-may-soon-become-sexy-suicide-coach-openai-advisor-reportedly-warned/))*

According to internal documents obtained by Ars Technica, OpenAI's own mental health expert advisory panel unanimously opposed the launch of a "naughty mode" for ChatGPT. One advisor warned in an internal memo that "if an AI combines sexually explicit content with emotional intimacy, it could effectively become a suicide coach for vulnerable users."

OpenAI proceeded with the launch despite the warning. The company drew a distinction between "AI adult content" and "pornography," stating the latter would remain prohibited — but experts argued that this line is ill-defined and unenforceable.

The episode throws fuel on the debate over whether AI companies must establish their own safety standards ahead of regulation. <span class="hl-red">A structure that dismisses internal expert warnings</span> starkly illustrates the tension between short-term profit motives and long-term safety obligations.

## Britannica vs. OpenAI: "ChatGPT Memorized Our Encyclopedia"

Encyclopedia Britannica and dictionary publisher Merriam-Webster have jointly sued OpenAI for copyright and trademark infringement. They allege that OpenAI used their copyrighted content without authorization to train its AI, and that ChatGPT generates responses that are "substantially similar" to their content.

They also raised trademark infringement claims, arguing that ChatGPT replicates Britannica's and Merriam-Webster's distinctive editorial style and phrasing — misleading users into believing they are receiving information directly from the original sources.

The lawsuit follows a broader wave of litigation from legacy media and content companies — including The New York Times, Condé Nast, and Getty Images — against OpenAI. The core legal question is <span class="hl-red">whether copyright infringement during AI training constitutes fair use</span>.

## A Fundamental Shift in the Legal Landscape for AI

Taken together, these three cases signal that the legal terrain facing AI companies is undergoing a fundamental transformation.

| Case | Plaintiff | Legal Issue | Potential Impact |
|------|-----------|-------------|------------------|
| xAI Grok CSAM | 3 minors | <span class="hl-red">Child protection, liability for illegal content generation</span> | Possible mandated AI safety filters |
| OpenAI adult mode | Internal advisor whistleblower | Corporate internal governance | Self-regulation failure → heightened external regulation |
| Britannica lawsuit | Britannica & Merriam-Webster | <span class="hl-red">Copyright and trademark infringement</span> | Formation of a licensing fee structure for AI training data |

<span class="hl-blue">In the long run, a licensing framework in which AI companies pay royalties for training data</span> is increasingly likely to take shape. This would raise the cost of AI model development, but could also create a foundation for a mutually beneficial relationship with the content industry.

## Outlook

The scope of legal liability for AI companies continues to expand. If the Grok lawsuit is certified as a class action, xAI could face billions of dollars in damages. OpenAI's adult mode, with internal dissent now leaking publicly, will draw the attention of regulators. The Britannica lawsuit may drag on for years, but it will serve as a benchmark for similar cases to follow. The legal growing pains of the AI industry have now begun in earnest.
