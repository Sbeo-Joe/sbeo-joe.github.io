The <span class="hl-red">Strait of Hormuz instability</span> caused by the Iran War is sending its first direct threat signals to the global semiconductor supply chain. Bloomberg reported that Iran War chokepoints have begun casting doubt on global chip supply, while Reuters ran an exclusive the same day revealing that China's second-largest foundry has entered preparations for <mark>7nm</mark> mass production.

## Key Takeaways

- Strait of Hormuz instability: the risk of disruption to semiconductor raw material shipping routes has emerged for the first time as a <span class="hl-red">global chip supply chain risk</span>
- China's No. 2 foundry (presumed to be SMIC) enters 7nm process mass production readiness — Beijing's semiconductor self-sufficiency drive accelerates
- <span class="hl-blue">Supply chain diversification beneficiaries</span>: the Iran War provides further justification for tightening U.S. semiconductor export controls against China

## How the Strait of Hormuz Threatens Semiconductors

Semiconductor manufacturing is not simply a process of making chips. It rests on a global logistics network that procures raw materials and specialty chemicals from around the world, ships them to TSMC, Samsung, and Intel fabs, and then dispatches the finished chips back across the globe.

The <span class="hl-red">Strait of Hormuz</span> is one of the critical arteries of this network. Approximately <mark>20%</mark> of the world's oil shipments pass through this strait. Rising oil prices translate into higher electricity costs, which directly drives up operating expenses for the power-intensive semiconductor fabs. There are even more direct pathways at risk. Some specialty gases and chemicals used in semiconductor manufacturing are shipped via routes that pass through the Middle East. Iran's attacks on the UAE's Fujairah port and Dubai airport demonstrate that these routes are already under threat.

> "The ripple effects of the Iran War have begun to extend beyond the military domain, targeting the heart of the global advanced technology supply chain." — Bloomberg, March 2026

## China's No. 2 Foundry Takes on the 7nm Challenge

According to Reuters' exclusive report, China's second-largest foundry (presumed to be SMIC, or Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation) has entered preparations for <mark>7nm</mark> process mass production. This is a continuation of Beijing's push to strengthen semiconductor self-sufficiency.

7nm is the process node used in Apple's A12 Bionic (2018) and early NVIDIA A100 chips. Compared to TSMC's current leading-edge 2–3nm processes, a significant gap remains — but it <span class="hl-blue">delivers sufficient performance for military, telecommunications, and AI inference chips</span>. If China becomes capable of independently mass-producing 7nm chips, fundamental questions arise about the effectiveness of U.S. export controls.

| Process Node | Current Use | China Status |
|---|---|---|
| 28nm | Automotive & industrial MCUs | <span class="hl-blue">Self-sufficient</span> |
| 14nm | Entry-level smartphones & servers | <span class="hl-blue">Mostly self-sufficient</span> |
| 7nm | AI inference & telecom chips | <span class="hl-red">Entering readiness</span> |
| 3–2nm | Cutting-edge AI & mobile | <span class="hl-red">Gap remains</span> |

## Geopolitical Crossroads: The Link Between the Iran War and China's Semiconductors

The two news stories appear separate on the surface, but they are structurally connected. The Iran War raises the pressure on the United States to compel allies to join the realignment of anti-China supply chains. South Korea, Japan, and Taiwan are simultaneously receiving requests to deploy forces to the Hormuz region while being pressured to choose sides in a U.S.-led semiconductor supply chain realignment.

From China's perspective, the Iran War is also an opportunity. While the United States and Europe are focused on the Middle East, Beijing can quietly build its semiconductor self-sufficiency capabilities. Just as North Korea reaped an estimated <mark>$14.4 billion</mark> in military support revenues from the Russia-Ukraine war, China too will exploit this turmoil strategically.

## Winners and Losers

<span class="hl-blue">Winners</span>: Intel Foundry, which is pushing to expand domestic semiconductor production in the United States; TSMC's Arizona fab, which receives U.S. government subsidies; and inland supply chain companies with low dependence on the Middle East.

<span class="hl-red">Losers</span>: Fabs that depend on Middle Eastern raw materials and chemicals; South Korean and Japanese semiconductor companies with high shares of finished-product exports through the Hormuz; and mid-node foundries whose market position is threatened by China's entry into 7nm.

## Outlook

The geopolitical fragmentation of semiconductor supply chains has been underway for some time, but the Iran War could act as a major catalyst to dramatically accelerate that pace. Whether China succeeds in 7nm mass production, and how quickly the Strait of Hormuz stabilizes, are the key variables for the semiconductor industry over the next 12 months. One thing is certain: in the semiconductor supply chain, geopolitics is no longer background noise.
