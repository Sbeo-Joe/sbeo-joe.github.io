Meta's AI smart glasses (a Ray-Ban collaboration) have been sending <span class="hl-red">user-recorded footage to outsourced workers in Kenya for review</span>, according to a published investigation. The findings triggered a class action lawsuit in San Francisco federal court.

## Key Takeaways

- Meta AI glasses footage routed to Kenyan contract workers for review — including nude and sexually explicit content
- Plaintiffs: Meta deceived consumers with advertising that promised privacy protection and sharing control
- Meta: data handling is within its terms of service — legal battle expected

## What Happened

Meta's Ray-Ban smart glasses feature an AI-powered camera that can record what the user sees. A joint investigation by Swedish outlets Svenska Dagbladet and Göteborgs-Posten found that portions of this footage were transmitted to outsourced workers in Nairobi, Kenya, for review. The report states the footage included <span class="hl-red">sensitive content, including nudity and sexually explicit material</span>.

The review purpose is quality control for AI model training data. Human review of AI-misprocessed footage or suspected policy-violating content is a practice common across the AI industry — OpenAI, Google, and Amazon run similar outsourced review systems. But the nature of the device — **glasses that record a user's daily life** — significantly amplifies the severity of the privacy implications.

## Core Legal Issues

The legal team behind the class action is using Meta's own marketing as a central exhibit. Meta ran advertising for its AI glasses touting user control over footage sharing. Plaintiffs argue this constitutes <span class="hl-red">false advertising</span>.

Meta's position is that the data handling is part of procedures disclosed in the terms of service. But the terms-of-service defense does not always hold in consumer courts — particularly when the advertising message and the terms of service contradict each other.

| Issue | Meta's Position | Plaintiffs' Position |
|-------|------------------|-----------------------|
| Data handling | Normal procedure within ToS | Deception contradicting advertising |
| User control | Can be limited via settings | No meaningful control |
| Outsourced review | Industry standard practice | Severity amplified by glasses form factor |

## The Wearable AI Privacy Dilemma

This lawsuit raises a larger question: when an **always-worn device** carries an AI camera, who governs that data and how? Unlike a smartphone, glasses can capture surroundings even when the user is not consciously recording. Footage from inside homes, hospital waiting rooms, and more private spaces can flow somewhere users cannot fully track.

<span class="hl-blue">Apple Vision Pro and Google Glass successors</span> are intensifying the wearable AI device race. The outcome of this lawsuit could rewrite the industry's data handling standards. Regulatory scrutiny is expected to increase — especially under Europe's GDPR framework, which would apply significantly stricter standards to this situation.
