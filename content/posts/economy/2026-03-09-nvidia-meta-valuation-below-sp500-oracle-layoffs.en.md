NVIDIA and Meta's valuations have dropped below the S&P 500's overall average. Meanwhile, reports surfaced that Oracle could lay off <mark>up to 30,000 workers</mark> to pour capital into AI data centers. The structural reshuffling of AI Big Tech is now showing up in hard numbers.

## Key Takeaways

- NVIDIA and Meta's price-to-earnings ratios (P/E) have fallen below the S&P 500 average of roughly `22x` — a valuation inversion
- Oracle is reportedly considering cutting up to <mark>30,000 jobs</mark> to fund AI data-center investments — a zero-sum trade-off between AI spending and headcount
- The Iran conflict, tariff concerns, and delayed rate cuts are collectively pressuring growth-stock valuations

## The Valuation Inversion — How It Happened

**NVIDIA** and **Meta Platforms** surged over the past two to three years as prime AI beneficiaries, commanding steep valuation premiums. But recent price corrections have wiped out that premium entirely. With the S&P 500's average P/E hovering around `22x`, both stocks now trade below that benchmark.

Does "cheaper" mean "opportunity" or "warning sign"? Opinions are split.

<span class="hl-blue">The bull case</span>: If earnings growth remains robust while valuations compress, it creates an attractive entry point. NVIDIA benefits from structurally rising AI chip demand, and Meta is seeing tangible returns from its advertising revenue recovery and AI platform investments.

<span class="hl-red">The bear case</span>: If the lower P/E reflects downward revisions to earnings estimates rather than just share-price declines, the picture changes dramatically. The Iran conflict, tariff uncertainty, and weakening consumer sentiment could all dent advertising and cloud demand, potentially breaking the earnings trajectory.

## Oracle's 30,000-Job Cut — The Reality of AI's Cost Structure

Reports emerged that **Oracle** is considering laying off up to <mark>30,000 employees</mark> to free up capital for AI data-center investments. This would represent a significant share of the company's total workforce — a massive restructuring by any measure. Compounding the pressure, reports also indicated that major U.S. banks are pulling back from Oracle's AI infrastructure initiatives.

The implication is clear. Building AI data centers requires enormous capital, and that funding ultimately comes from headcount reductions or other cost cuts. The abstract debate about AI replacing jobs has now materialized into a concrete restructuring plan at a specific company.

> "To invest in AI, you have to cut somewhere. Oracle chose to cut people."

## Investment Implications

Read together, these two stories deliver a single message: the AI investment cycle is continuing, but the way companies fund it and monetize it is becoming far more pragmatic — and pressured.

Key scenarios for investors to watch:
- 🔑 **NVIDIA and Meta's valuation appeal is rising** — for those who can stomach short-term volatility, the long-term holding thesis is strengthening
- ⚠️ **If Oracle-style layoffs spread across Big Tech**, the cascading impact on IT-sector employment and consumer sentiment could be significant
- 📌 **Individual stock selection matters more than broad AI cloud/infrastructure ETFs** in this environment

As the market digests Iran-conflict uncertainty and recalibrates rate-path expectations, the direction of Big Tech valuations will likely hinge on <mark>Q2 2026 earnings season</mark> as a decisive turning point.
