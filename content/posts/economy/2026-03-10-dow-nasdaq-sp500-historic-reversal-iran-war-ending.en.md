The Dow Jones Industrial Average swung nearly <mark>700 points</mark> from its session low to close in positive territory on Monday, March 9, after President Trump declared the Iran conflict "Very Complete" — detonating a market reversal that sent oil from near <mark>$120</mark> to below <mark>$100</mark> per barrel in a single session and triggered a broad recovery across all three major US indices.

## Key Takeaways

- The Dow closed up over <mark>+200 points</mark> after being down more than 500 points intraday — a swing of nearly 700 points in a single session
- Crude oil plunged after Trump announced sanctions relief and signaled the Iran conflict was approaching resolution, with the G-7 signaling readiness to tap strategic reserves
- Semiconductor stocks led the recovery, with the <mark>3x leveraged semiconductor ETF</mark> surging over `+11%` as the war-premium compression played out fastest in chip supply-chain-sensitive names

## The Anatomy of a Reversal

Monday's session began as a continuation of the multi-week selloff triggered by the Iran conflict. Dow futures had fallen, oil was still elevated, and the dominant market narrative was that geopolitical risk premiums would stay embedded in equities until a clear diplomatic resolution emerged.

Then Trump spoke.

The president's statement that the Iran war was "Very Complete" — later clarified to mean the conflict was approaching a definitive end — hit the market like a release valve. Within minutes of the remarks, crude futures reversed sharply. The G-7 issued a coordinated statement declaring readiness to release strategic petroleum reserves (SPR) if needed. Trump simultaneously announced sanctions relief on certain oil-related transactions, easing supply concerns.

The cascade was immediate:

| Asset | Session Low | Close | Swing |
|-------|------------|-------|-------|
| Dow Jones | −500+ pts | +200 pts | ~700 pts |
| S&P 500 | −1.8% | +0.6% | ~2.4% |
| Nasdaq | −2.1% | +0.8% | ~2.9% |
| WTI Crude | ~$118 | ~$97 | −$21 |

The speed of the reversal was striking. Markets had priced in a prolonged conflict; when that assumption cracked, the de-risking unwound almost as fast as it had built.

## Why Semiconductors Led

The <span class="hl-blue">semiconductor sector</span> was the standout winner of the session. The 3x leveraged semiconductor ETF gained over `+11.6%` on Monday — the most in the equity complex. The logic is straightforward: chips are the sector most exposed to Iran-war supply-chain premiums via their dependence on rare materials, energy-intensive fabrication, and global logistics networks that had been disrupted.

When war premium compresses, it compresses fastest where it was most embedded. Semiconductor stocks had fallen disproportionately during the Iran escalation, pricing in extended supply chain disruption. The reversal simply unwound that premium.

> "The old game is back — oil is once again a potent geopolitical tool. And when it reverses, everything reverses with it."

## The Structural Picture: Where Markets Stand Now

One session does not erase weeks of damage. The S&P 500 remains in a technical correction from its pre-war highs. JP Morgan had warned that a sustained Iran conflict could push the S&P 500 down a further `10%` from then-current levels — that scenario is now less likely but not fully priced out.

Morgan Stanley's analysis, circulating during Monday's session, noted that markets were "closer to the end of this rolling correction" — a phrase that now carries more weight after Trump's statement. The key variables for the days ahead:

- **Confirmation**: Does the ceasefire or diplomatic resolution actually hold? Markets are trading Trump's words, not a signed agreement.
- **Oil normalization**: WTI crude needs to sustain below $100 for the inflation/Fed calculus to stabilize. One session's move is not a trend.
- **Earnings guidance**: Companies in travel, energy, and consumer sectors that revised guidance during the Iran escalation will need to re-guide again.

## The Fed Dimension

The Iran conflict had complicated the Federal Reserve's rate path in a specific way: energy-driven inflation threatened to reignite CPI prints just as the Fed was considering rate cuts following the weak February jobs report (`−92,000` jobs). A sustained oil price decline removes that complication.

<span class="hl-blue">If oil sustains below $100</span>, the disinflationary impulse could actually accelerate the Fed's cutting cycle — a dynamic that would be additionally positive for equities, particularly rate-sensitive sectors like real estate and utilities that had been doubly punished by both Iran fears and sticky-rate concerns.

<span class="hl-red">If the Iran situation re-escalates</span>, the Monday reversal becomes a dead-cat bounce and the prior lows would be tested again rapidly.

## Winners and Losers from the Reversal

<span class="hl-blue">Winners</span>: Semiconductor stocks, airlines (fuel cost relief), cruise lines, consumer discretionary, REITs, long-duration bond holders (as rate cut probability rises), and any company that had been hit by war-risk premium rather than fundamental deterioration.

<span class="hl-red">Losers</span>: Energy sector longs who had positioned for sustained $110+ oil, volatility traders who were short the VIX, and investors who panic-sold during the worst of the correction — the fastest reversals tend to leave reactive sellers behind.

## What to Watch

The key near-term signposts are: an official diplomatic statement confirming the conflict's end, WTI crude sustaining below $100, and the Fed's next communication on rate cut timing in light of improved energy dynamics. If all three align, Monday's session may be remembered as the inflection point of the 2026 market correction. If any one of them disappoints, the re-test of session lows remains on the table.
