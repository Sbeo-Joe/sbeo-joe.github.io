Meta has allocated up to <mark>$135 billion</mark> in AI-related capital expenditure (capex) for <mark>2026</mark>, while signing a <mark>$27 billion</mark> deal with AI infrastructure firm Nebius. Nebius shares surged <mark>+14%</mark> immediately following the news. Yet on the same day, prominent Silicon Valley investor Bill Gurley publicly warned, "Fast money ran the show, and a reset is coming," cautioning of an AI bubble collapse.

## Key Takeaways

- Meta AI capex up to <mark>$135B</mark> — $27B infrastructure deal signed with Nebius, Nebius stock surges <mark>+14%</mark>
- Meta plans to pair its AI spending with <span class="hl-red">large-scale layoffs</span> to offset cost concerns — using cuts to reassure investors
- <span class="hl-red">Bill Gurley's warning</span>: "Fast money has already created a bubble in the AI venture ecosystem, and a reset is coming soon"

## Meta's $135 Billion AI Bet

<mark>$135 billion</mark>. This number needs context. Meta's total revenue in 2025 was approximately <mark>$164 billion</mark>. In other words, Meta plans to deploy AI infrastructure investment equivalent to nearly its entire annual revenue in just the single year of 2026.

Investor reaction was mixed. Meta's share price initially fell after the announcement, then rebounded nearly <mark>+3%</mark> once accompanying layoff plans were disclosed alongside the AI spending figures. The market appeared to weigh the commitment to cost control more heavily than the scale of the AI investment itself.

## Nebius: Meta's Chosen AI Infrastructure Partner

Nebius is an AI infrastructure and cloud company spun off from the Russian technology firm Yandex. This <mark>$27 billion</mark> deal is the largest in Nebius's history. Meta plans to leverage Nebius's GPU clusters and data center infrastructure to rapidly scale AI model training and inference capacity.

The rationale for choosing Nebius comes down to speed and cost. Existing hyperscalers such as AWS, Azure, and GCP are not agile enough to absorb large-scale AI workloads on Meta's timeline, and in the short term it is more efficient to engage a specialized AI infrastructure provider than to build from scratch.

## Bill Gurley's Warning: "A Reset Is Coming"

<span class="hl-red">Bill Gurley</span> delivered a sharp warning about the AI investment ecosystem in a CNBC interview. An early investor in Uber and a partner at Benchmark Capital, he is one of the most influential voices in Silicon Valley.

His message was unambiguous: "AI startups have raised too much money, too fast. Massive capital has flooded in before product-market fit has been validated, and this will inevitably lead to a reset." He also drew parallels to the dot-com bubble collapse of 2000.

Gurley's scrutiny extended to Anthropic and OpenAI as well. He described the way these companies operate as "scary" — structures in which a sense of mission outweighs commercial accountability, and in which investment frameworks absorb near-infinite losses, are simply not sustainable, he argued.

> "A bunch of people got rich quick. And a reset is coming." — Bill Gurley, CNBC interview

## Conflicting Signals: Who Is Right?

<span class="hl-blue">The optimistic case</span>: Meta's $135 billion commitment and the $27 billion Nebius deal demonstrate that demand for AI infrastructure is real, not illusory. Nvidia's trillion-dollar order backlog points in the same direction. As long as genuine demand underpins the numbers, the bubble argument loses force.

<span class="hl-red">The pessimistic case</span>: As Gurley points out, the pace at which infrastructure investment converts into actual revenue remains deeply uncertain. There is still no clear validation of how much Meta — spending $135 billion — can realistically earn from AI. Whether the valuations of AI infrastructure companies like Nebius are sustainable also remains an open question.

## Outlook

The fact that Meta's massive AI bet and Gurley's warning arrived on the same day suggests the AI investment cycle has reached an inflection point. How Meta deploys its $135 billion — and the extent to which results materialize as advertising, metaverse, and AI services revenue — will be a defining indicator for the tech sector in the second half of 2026.
