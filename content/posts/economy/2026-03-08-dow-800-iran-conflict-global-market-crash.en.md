<mark>−800 points</mark>. On <mark>March 5, 2026</mark>, the Dow Jones Industrial Average dropped by that much in a single trading day. As international oil prices broke through <mark>$80</mark> per barrel, the economic shockwaves from the Iran conflict turned all too real.

## Key Takeaways

- The Dow's `~800-point` plunge pushed its <mark>2026 year-to-date return</mark> into negative territory for the first time
- Surging oil prices driven by the Iran conflict reignited inflation fears, sending U.S. Treasury yields to a `3-week high`
- Australia's stock market lost <mark>$91B</mark> in a single week; South Korea and broader Asian markets saw extreme volatility

## 🔥 Oil Price Surge as the Trigger

As the Iran conflict entered a prolonged phase, international oil prices broke through <mark>$80</mark> per barrel. Investors read this as a sign that a regional conflict was shaking global commodity markets, and quickly moved to cut exposure to risk assets.

Wall Street's reaction was swift and severe. The Dow plunged nearly <span class="hl-red">`800 points`</span>, dragging the S&P 500 and Nasdaq down with it. The sell-off pushed the Dow into <mark>negative year-to-date territory for 2026</mark> — a first for the year.

> "Geopolitical crisis is driving the market. This is a market where headlines, not economic fundamentals, are setting prices."

## 📊 Global Market Snapshot

| Market | Status |
|--------|--------|
| U.S. Dow Jones | `−800 points` (YTD turns negative) |
| S&P 500 | Decline, ongoing daily swings of 1–2% |
| Nasdaq | Fell in tandem |
| Australia ASX | Lost <mark>$91B</mark> in one week |
| South Korea KOSPI | Extreme volatility |
| South Korean Won | ~<mark>2-month low</mark> vs. the dollar |

Australia's stock market shed <mark>$91 billion (approx. 130 trillion KRW)</mark> in a single week, as the Iran conflict fallout collided with the tail end of earnings season. South Korea's KOSPI swung wildly, and the won hit a two-month low against the dollar.

## 💡 The Return of Inflation Fear

The oil price surge is far more than a simple rise in energy costs. U.S. Treasury yields climbed to a <span class="hl-red">3-week high</span>, and the average mortgage rate bounced back above the `6%` mark. This adds significant pressure on the Federal Reserve heading into its March meeting, further dimming hopes for a near-term rate cut.

The Fed had already been slowing the pace of rate reductions this year. If an oil shock fans the flames of inflation, <span class="hl-red">the rate-cut cycle could be pushed back even further.</span> Markets wasted no time pricing in exactly that scenario.

## 📌 Market Irony: Broadcom Bucks the Trend

The lone bright spot of the day was Broadcom. CEO **Hock Tan** delivered a forceful message during the earnings call — "AI growth continues" — and amid the gloom blanketing the semiconductor sector, <span class="hl-blue">Broadcom stood alone in the green</span>. It was widely interpreted as a signal that the AI infrastructure investment thesis remains intact even amid geopolitical chaos.

## Outlook

Unless clear signs of a ceasefire emerge from the Iran conflict, oil prices and market volatility are likely to stay elevated for the foreseeable future. The U.S. Nonfarm Payrolls report due Friday will be the critical data point shaping the Fed's next move. In an environment where geopolitical uncertainty and inflationary pressure coexist, all eyes are on how investors choose to position themselves.
