The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) is weighing <span class="hl-red">the elimination of mandatory quarterly earnings reporting</span>. A system that has served as the bedrock of American capital markets for <mark>over 90 years</mark> since the <mark>1934</mark> Great Depression is now being called into question.

## Key Takeaways

- SEC reviewing relaxation of quarterly earnings reporting (10-Q) obligations for listed companies — an extension of the Trump administration's deregulatory agenda
- Inevitable structural overhaul of the quarterly earnings ecosystem (analysts, IR departments, media, law firms, data providers) worth hundreds of billions of dollars
- "Promoting long-term investment" vs. "worsening information asymmetry" — the core of the debate

## What Is the Quarterly Earnings Reporting System?

Under the Securities Exchange Act, U.S. publicly listed companies are required to file 10-Q reports with the SEC every quarter. This system — mandating disclosure of a company's financial condition and operating performance four times a year, every <mark>90 days</mark> — was built on the hard lessons of the Great Depression of <mark>1934</mark>.

However, amid the Trump administration's sweeping deregulatory push, criticism of this requirement has grown louder. The argument is that quarterly reporting forces executives to <span class="hl-red">obsess over short-term results</span>, ultimately undermining long-term R&D and capital investment. Major developed economies — including the UK, Germany, and Japan — already operate on semi-annual (6-month) or annual reporting frameworks.

## The Ecosystem at Stake

The quarterly earnings reporting system underpins a vast industrial ecosystem that extends far beyond simple paperwork.

| Sector | Role | Scale of Impact |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| Equity analysts | Drafting and distributing EPS estimates | Tens of thousands of Wall Street jobs |
| IR (Investor Relations) departments | Earnings preparation and roadshows | Dozens of staff per major firm |
| Financial media | Earnings coverage and commentary | Key source of advertising and subscription revenue |
| Law firms and accounting firms | Auditing and litigation defense | Tens of billions of dollars annually |
| Data providers | Bloomberg, FactSet, Refinitiv | Billions of dollars in subscription fees |

If this ecosystem contracts significantly, <span class="hl-red">white-collar employment across the financial sector would decline</span> and the entire information distribution infrastructure of Wall Street would be restructured. Business Insider described it as "a shock that would rattle thousands of lawyers, communications professionals, and data providers."

## The Core of the Debate

> "If the quarterly reporting obligation disappears, Wall Street would lose a pacemaker that has kept its heart beating every 90 days."

**Arguments for abolition**: Freed from short-term earnings pressure, executives could reallocate capital toward long-term investment horizons of 5–10 years. Amazon's willingness to absorb quarterly losses in its early years in pursuit of long-term growth, and Berkshire Hathaway's long-term compounding strategy, are cited as prime examples. Additionally, reduced compliance costs would ease the burden on small- and mid-cap listed companies.

**Arguments against abolition**: Information asymmetry would worsen. Without quarterly reports, the information gap between institutional investors and retail investors would widen further. Earnings Season is a key driver of market liquidity — its disappearance could reduce overall market efficiency. MarketWatch also raised a practical counterargument: "Even without the mandate, companies will likely continue reporting quarterly on a voluntary basis."

## Winners and Losers

<span class="hl-blue">Potential beneficiaries</span>: Growth companies with long-term investment visions (tech, biotech), CFOs who have long endured share price volatility around earnings seasons, and long-horizon hedge funds.

<span class="hl-red">Potential losers</span>: Financial media outlets with earnings-season-centric revenue models, the EPS estimate industry as a whole, and trading desks that profit from short-term volatility.

## Outlook

Any SEC rule change must pass through a Public Comment Period and is expected to face significant congressional pushback. Actual regulatory change will take considerable time. That said, with the Trump administration's deregulatory stance firmly established, the possibility of a concrete rule revision emerging in <mark>late 2026 to 2027</mark> cannot be ruled out.

Whether a <mark>90-year-old</mark> institution is truly on the verge of transformation — or whether this deregulatory discussion will amount to little more than a passing episode — has become the pivotal variable that will determine the future structure of U.S. capital markets.
