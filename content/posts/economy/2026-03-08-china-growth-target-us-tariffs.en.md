China unveiled its <mark>lowest economic growth target since 1991</mark> at the <mark>2026 National People's Congress (NPC)</mark>, on the same day U.S. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent confirmed that global baseline tariffs would be raised from the current `10%` to <mark>`15%`</mark> — with the move set to take effect this week.

## Key Takeaways

- China's growth target set at the <mark>lowest level since 1991</mark> — reflecting the triple pressure of tariff headwinds, weak domestic demand, and Iran-driven energy shocks
- U.S. global baseline tariff hike from `10%` to <mark>`15%`</mark> confirmed for this week by Treasury Secretary Bessent
- <span class="hl-red">Iran war-induced inflation + tariff pressure</span> hitting simultaneously — Bloomberg warns of "stagflation risk"

## China's Low-Growth Target — What It Really Signals

China setting its lowest growth target since 1991 is more than a matter of numbers. It signals that a Chinese economy already battered by a real estate crisis, sluggish consumption, and slowing exports in 2024–2025 has abandoned any hopes of a high-growth rebound in 2026.

There is also a strategic logic to setting a conservative target. Politically, it is far better to modestly exceed a cautious goal than to fall short of an ambitious one. But at the same time, the move signals that Beijing itself recognizes the economic environment this year will be anything but forgiving.

> "China setting such a low growth target is not mere humility. It reflects a realistic assessment of what's coming — Trump tariffs, Iran-driven energy shocks, and weak domestic demand all hitting at once." — The Edge Malaysia

## Trump's 15% Tariff — Confirmed for This Week

Treasury Secretary Bessent confirmed that the global baseline tariff increase from `10%` to `15%` will proceed as scheduled this week. Separately, a U.S. trade court ruled that importers are entitled to refunds on previously invalidated Trump-era tariffs.

| Tariff Measure | Details |
|---|---|
| Current global baseline tariff | `10%` |
| Planned hike this week | <mark>`15%`</mark> |
| Additional China-specific tariffs | Under separate negotiation |
| Trade court refund ruling | Refunds possible on previously voided tariffs |
| War-driven price pressure | Risk of amplifying tariff impact |

This move is not aimed at China alone. The `15%` global baseline tariff applies to all major trading partners — including South Korea, Japan, and Europe — raising the cost structure across global supply chains.

## The Double Shock — Tariffs + Iran War Inflation

Bloomberg has warned that rising energy prices driven by the Iran conflict will deliver an additional inflationary blow to a global economy already strained by tariffs. Analysts are increasingly viewing <span class="hl-red">stagflation</span> — the combination of economic stagnation and rising prices — as a real and growing risk.

- <span class="hl-red">Energy price surge</span>: Iran conflict → higher oil and gas prices → broad increase in production costs
- <span class="hl-red">Tariff pressure</span>: Higher import prices → rising consumer inflation
- <span class="hl-blue">Relative winners</span>: Energy-independent economies (U.S. shale, non-conflict Middle East) gain a competitive edge
- <span class="hl-blue">Gold rally</span>: Geopolitical uncertainty driving gold prices higher — safe-haven demand surging

Hong Kong's Hang Seng Index (HSI) fell <mark>-1.1%</mark> on the day amid the oil price shock and geopolitical risk, while global bond markets also experienced significant turbulence.

## What Comes Next — The Era of Slowing Global Trade

The simultaneous unfolding of China's low-growth target, U.S. tariff hikes, and Iran-driven energy shocks marks an environment fundamentally different from the era of expanding global free trade that defined the 2010s. This looks increasingly like a structural transition — one defined by shrinking trade volumes, supply chain realignment, and persistent inflationary pressure.

For investors, <span class="hl-blue">energy-independent companies, firms benefiting from supply chain localization, and gold and commodity-linked assets</span> may attract near-term attention. Conversely, <span class="hl-red">companies heavily reliant on Chinese exports and manufacturers sensitive to energy costs</span> face the prospect of additional headwinds. Where global trade finds its new equilibrium is the defining question — and the next six months will be critical to watch.
