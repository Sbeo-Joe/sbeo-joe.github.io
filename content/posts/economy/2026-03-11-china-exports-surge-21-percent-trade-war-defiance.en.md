China's exports surged <mark>21.8%</mark> in January–February 2026 compared to the same period last year — a result that directly contradicts the narrative that US tariffs are successfully reducing Chinese export capacity, and signals that global supply chains have adapted faster than policymakers anticipated.

## Key Takeaways

- China's <mark>+21.8%</mark> Jan-Feb export growth is the largest two-month surge in years, driven by record shipments to Southeast Asia, Europe, and the Middle East that bypass US tariff barriers
- China's chip exports alone grew <mark>73%</mark> year-over-year, reflecting the acceleration of domestic semiconductor production and the rerouting of chip sales to non-US markets
- The data represents a structural challenge to the US tariff strategy: it suggests China has successfully rerouted trade flows and may be capturing market share from US-aligned suppliers in third-country markets

## 🌏 The Rerouting Story Behind the Headline

The <mark>21.8%</mark> headline obscures where the growth is coming from. US-China direct bilateral trade has declined — but China has systematically expanded exports through Southeast Asian manufacturing hubs (Vietnam, Malaysia, Indonesia), Middle Eastern distribution centers, and directly to European markets that have not aligned with US tariff frameworks.

This is the "tariff arbitrage" that trade economists warned about: when tariffs target one bilateral trade corridor, they incentivize rerouting through third countries rather than reducing total trade volume. The data suggests China has executed this rerouting efficiently over the past 18–24 months.

For context:

| Trade Flow | Direction |
|-----------|-----------|
| China → US direct | 📉 Declining |
| China → ASEAN → US | 📈 Increasing |
| China → EU | 📈 Increasing |
| China → Middle East | 📈 Increasing |
| China → Global total | 📈 +21.8% YoY |

## 💡 The Semiconductor Surge: A Critical Signal

The <mark>73%</mark> surge in China's chip exports deserves separate analysis. This growth reflects two simultaneous dynamics.

First, **domestic production maturation**: China's chip industry has been investing heavily in capacity since the US export controls on advanced semiconductors began in 2022–2023. While China cannot yet produce cutting-edge <mark>3nm</mark> or <mark>5nm</mark> chips at the performance level of TSMC's most advanced nodes, it has dramatically scaled production of mature-node chips (28nm and above) that serve the vast majority of global electronics demand — automotive, industrial, consumer electronics.

Second, **market share capture**: As US-aligned chip suppliers have faced export licensing complexity and geopolitical uncertainty, some buyers in developing markets have pivoted to Chinese-manufactured alternatives. The mature-node chip market — worth hundreds of billions of dollars annually — is where China is now winning market share aggressively.

<span class="hl-red">The risk for US chip companies</span> like Texas Instruments and analog semiconductor manufacturers is significant. Mature-node chips have been a reliable, high-margin business. Chinese competition in this segment is not a future threat — it's a present one.

## 📊 Trade War Scorecard, 2026

Six-plus years into the US-China trade conflict, the data suggests a more complex outcome than either side's original thesis:

- <span class="hl-blue">US wins</span>: Advanced AI chip access (H100, Blackwell) has been restricted; China cannot domestically produce frontier AI training hardware yet
- <span class="hl-red">US setbacks</span>: Total Chinese export volume growing at 21.8%; mature-node chip market share shifting; Southeast Asian rerouting undermining bilateral tariff intent
- <span class="hl-blue">China wins</span>: Domestic semiconductor ecosystem developing; alternative trade routes established; export volume growing despite tariff environment
- <span class="hl-red">China constraints</span>: Advanced semiconductor capability gap remains; limited access to Dutch lithography equipment (ASML EUV) for cutting-edge production

## ⚖️ Implications for Global Supply Chains

The structural implication of the export surge data is that the global manufacturing map is being redrawn — not reversed. Supply chains are not returning to a pre-2018 configuration, but they are also not cleanly bifurcating into US-aligned and China-aligned blocs. Instead, a complex web of triangular trade relationships is emerging, with Southeast Asia as the critical intermediary.

<span class="hl-blue">Winners</span> in this environment: Southeast Asian manufacturers capturing value-added production, logistics companies handling increased transit trade, and Chinese exporters who have successfully built diversified customer bases.

<span class="hl-red">Losers</span>: Trade policy advocates who assumed tariffs would force manufacturing reshoring to the US, and US manufacturers in mature-tech sectors now facing intensified Chinese competition in third markets.

> "The tariff wall didn't stop the river. It just made the river find a new route."

The most important number to watch in the next quarter is whether ASEAN export growth to the US accelerates in parallel with China's direct export surge — that correlation would be the clearest evidence of systematic rerouting. If it does, the policy response question becomes: do tariffs follow the goods through the rerouting, or does the arbitrage persist?
