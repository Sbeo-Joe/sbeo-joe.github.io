Nissan will begin exporting Murano SUVs produced at its Smyrna, Tennessee plant to Japan starting in <mark>early 2027</mark>. This marks the first time American-made Nissan vehicles have been sold in the Japanese market since the <mark>1990s</mark>.

## Key Takeaways

- Nissan to export Tennessee-built Murano SUVs to Japan — first sale of American-made Nissans in Japan since the <mark>1990s</mark>
- Toyota and Honda pursuing the same strategy — all three Japanese automakers pivot to re-exporting US-produced vehicles back to Japan
- The paradox of responding to Trump tariffs — expanding US local production triggers a supply chain realignment that hollows out Japan's own manufacturing

## What Is Happening

The Trump administration has imposed <span class="hl-red">25–30% tariffs</span> on Japanese automobiles. Japan's three major automakers — Nissan, Toyota, and Honda — have responded by dramatically scaling up their US domestic production. Yet this strategy is playing out in an unexpected direction: a "reverse export" structure has taken shape in which cars manufactured in America are now being sold in Japan.

Nissan's Smyrna, Tennessee plant is one of the largest auto factories in North America, with an annual production capacity of <mark>640,000 vehicles</mark>. Since Trump's tariffs took effect, it has become economically more advantageous to produce and export from US facilities than from factories back in Japan.

Toyota has already announced plans to export Kentucky-built vehicles to Japan, while Honda is pursuing a plan to redirect a portion of its Ohio plant output toward Asian markets.

## The Paradox of the Trade War

Trump's tariffs ostensibly serve two goals: protecting American manufacturing jobs and reducing the trade deficit. Yet the response from Japan's three automakers reveals an unintended collision between these two objectives.

| Scenario | US Jobs | US Trade Deficit | Japanese Manufacturing |
|----------|---------|-----------------|------------------------|
| Import from Japan | No impact | Worsens | Maintained |
| US Local Production | Increases ✓ | Partial improvement ✓ | Hollowing begins |
| US-made → Re-export to Japan | Further increase | **Turns to surplus** | <span class="hl-red">Direct blow</span> |

If the structure of manufacturing in the US and selling to Japan becomes entrenched, it creates an optimal scenario for the United States — <span class="hl-blue">simultaneously improving the trade balance and boosting employment</span>. Meanwhile, domestic auto factories in Japan face declining production volumes and mounting employment threats.

> "Japanese companies that built factories in the US to escape tariffs are now using those very factories to target the Japanese market — a reversal even more dramatic than anything Trump had envisioned."

## Second- and Third-Order Effects of Supply Chain Realignment

**First-order effects**: Higher utilization rates at US auto plants and increased parts orders. Improved employment indicators across "auto belt" states such as Tennessee, Kentucky, and Ohio.

**Second-order effects**: Declining utilization rates at domestic Japanese auto plants. Employment pressures in Japanese automotive industry cities such as Toyota City (豊田市). A growing urgency for the Japanese government to develop countermeasures.

**Third-order effects**: Accelerated consideration of similar strategies by other major automotive export nations such as South Korea and Germany. Hyundai and Kia are already expanding US local production through their Georgia plant, and European automakers are likewise rushing to localize US manufacturing.

## Winners and Losers

<span class="hl-blue">Winners</span> are parts suppliers and logistics companies near US auto plants. As US local production grows, so does demand for locally sourced components. American consumers, however, may face <span class="hl-red">upward price pressure</span> in the short term, as fewer vehicles being re-exported to Japan could tighten domestic supply.

<span class="hl-red">Losers</span> are automotive suppliers within Japan. As domestic production by Nissan, Toyota, and Honda contracts, orders for Japan's tier-1 and tier-2 parts suppliers will fall in tandem. The Japanese government now finds itself under pressure to urgently craft industrial policy to cushion the employment shock.

Japanese-brand cars built in America, re-exported to Japan — whether this reverse-export trend proves a temporary phenomenon or a structural shift will be the defining storyline to watch in the global auto industry in <mark>2026–2027</mark>.
