The Iran war has already crashed stock markets and spiked crude oil prices. Now the <span class="hl-red">economic pain is spreading to consumers</span> — through grocery bills, mortgage payments, and goods prices — as the conflict's supply chain disruptions compound across the global economy.

## Key Takeaways

- **Maersk CEO** Vincent Clerc says Iran war shipping costs "will be passed on" to consumers; Maersk is the world's second-largest container shipping operator
- Iran conflict disrupts fertilizer shipments through the <mark>Strait of Hormuz</mark>, threatening a new wave of global food inflation
- G7 nations agreed to a <mark>record emergency release</mark> of oil reserves; mortgage rates hit their highest level since <mark>August 2025</mark>

## 🚢 Shipping: The Cost Pass-Through Begins

**Vincent Clerc**, chief executive of **Maersk**, delivered the most direct warning yet to consumers in an interview with BBC: the additional costs of rerouting vessels around the Strait of Hormuz and the Persian Gulf — longer journeys, higher insurance premiums, and increased security expenses — will ultimately be charged to the buyer of goods.

Maersk's calculation is straightforward. The Strait of Hormuz handles roughly `20%` of global oil shipments and a significant share of liquefied natural gas (LNG) and container cargo. The war has forced rerouting around southern Africa — adding <mark>10–14 days</mark> to transit times and `$800–$2,000` per container in additional costs. For a retail supply chain where margins run at `2–5%`, that premium cannot be absorbed indefinitely.

> "Someone has to pay for the war — and it won't be the shipping companies."

— **Vincent Clerc**, CEO, Maersk

## 🌾 Food Prices: The Fertilizer Shock Nobody Is Talking About

The more underreported story is fertilizer. The Persian Gulf and surrounding region is a critical corridor for ammonia, urea, and phosphate shipments — the raw inputs for agricultural fertilizers that underpin global food production.

With shipments disrupted and insurance costs for Gulf-transit cargo surging, agricultural commodity traders are already flagging a <span class="hl-red">supply squeeze in the spring planting window</span>. Global food prices, which had moderated through late 2025 after the 2022–2023 surge, face a renewed inflationary shock. The most exposed regions are <span class="hl-red">South Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa</span>, which import the majority of their fertilizer needs through Gulf shipping lanes.

## 🏠 Mortgage Markets: The War Reaches Main Street

In the United Kingdom, mortgage rates hit their highest level since <mark>August 2025</mark> as lenders pulled deals and repriced risk. The dynamics are familiar: oil-driven inflation expectations force bond markets to price in higher-for-longer interest rates, which translates directly into mortgage costs.

| Period | Average UK Mortgage Rate | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-war (Feb 2026) | `~4.2%` | Post-Bank of England cuts |
| Post-spike (Mar 2026) | `~5.1%+` | Highest since Aug 2025 |

The US Federal Reserve faces the same dilemma. Morgan Stanley economists have explicitly flagged risks that the oil shock will delay the next Fed rate cut — a reversal of market expectations that had priced in `2–3 cuts` in 2026. <span class="hl-red">Higher-for-longer rates in a war environment is a scenario most consumer portfolios are not positioned for.</span>

## 🛢️ Emergency Oil Reserves: A Record Response

In a coordinated response, G7 nations agreed to an <mark>unprecedented release</mark> of emergency strategic oil reserves — the largest in the IEA's (International Energy Agency) history. South Korea announced it would release <mark>22.46 million barrels</mark> under the IEA plan.

The mechanics of reserve releases create temporary price relief but do not resolve the underlying supply disruption. The Iran war has removed Iranian production from global markets and disrupted the physical movement of Gulf crude. Until a ceasefire or diplomatic resolution — which Maersk's Clerc explicitly called for — the structural supply gap remains.

The critical variable for consumers: oil above <mark>$90/barrel</mark> triggers a recognized feedback loop into transportation costs, food production, and goods prices that typically takes `3–6 months` to fully materialize at the retail level. That clock started in February 2026.
