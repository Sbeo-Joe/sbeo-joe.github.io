## 💰 120,000 Yen Per Month — The Reality of an "Ordinary" Retirement

*Shukatsu* (終活) — end-of-life preparation activities. Many people hear this word and think, "That's still far away." But one 64-year-old man's confession exposes the danger of that assumption.

<mark>120,000 yen</mark> monthly pension. After deductions, about <mark>100,000 yen</mark> take-home. After rent, utilities, and food, almost nothing remains. "When I was young, I thought it would work out somehow," he reflects. His <mark>3 million yen</mark> in savings could vanish with a single serious illness.

| Item | Monthly Amount |
|------|---------------|
| Pension (take-home) | ~<mark>100,000 yen</mark> |
| Rent | ~50,000 yen |
| Utilities & phone | ~15,000 yen |
| Food | ~25,000 yen |
| Remaining | ~<mark>10,000 yen</mark> |

> "I want to tell my pre-retirement self: start thinking about old age right now."

This is not an unusual story. Japan's average national pension is about <mark>56,000 yen/month</mark>; even including employer pension, the male average is about <mark>160,000 yen</mark>. An "ordinary" retirement is tougher than most imagine.

## 🌿 "Yuru-Shukatsu" — Preparing Your Heart Without Spending Money

Amid these concerns, a concept called <span class="hl-blue">"yuru-shukatsu" (ゆる終活, gentle end-of-life planning)</span> is gaining traction. Rather than elaborate wills or complex estate planning, you start with just **one sheet of paper**.

Three pillars of yuru-shukatsu:

- 📌 **Create a list of important people** — contact details plus words you want to share
- 📌 **Organize your digital legacy** — passwords, social media accounts, subscriptions
- 📌 **Write your medical wishes** — life support preferences, organ donation intent

Perfection isn't the goal. "Just write what you can write today." That is the essence of yuru-shukatsu.

## 📝 The Evolution of Ending Notes

Weekly Bunshun's March 19 issue features a "shukatsu and ending" special, while Asahi Shimbun is hosting free online <span class="hl-blue">ending note seminars</span>.

An **ending note** is a personal record of your life and final wishes. Unlike a legal will, it has no binding power — but it serves as a "last letter" to your family.

Modern ending notes are evolving. Digital versions allow smartphone input. Some people attach photos and videos, leaving messages of gratitude for their loved ones.

## 🙏 Lessons for All of Us

Shukatsu is not "preparing for death." It is **reviewing how you want to live**. How do you want to spend your remaining years? What do you want to say, and to whom? Thinking about these questions enriches today.

The 64-year-old man's regret wasn't only about money. It was about "putting off saying important things to important people." That regret can happen to anyone, at any age.

Why not pull out a sheet of paper today and write the name of someone who matters to you?
