## 🏥 The Surprising Truth About Neck Pain

When neck pain strikes, most people instinctively reach for the back of their neck. But what the surgeon recommends targeting is the **sternocleidomastoid (SCM)**, a powerful paired muscle running diagonally down the front and sides of the neck.

The SCM controls head rotation and forward flexion — exactly the movements we make constantly when looking down at a phone or leaning toward a computer. When this muscle becomes chronically contracted, the consequences extend well beyond neck discomfort. The SCM runs adjacent to the carotid artery; when it tightens, it can <span class="hl-red">contribute to headaches, shoulder pain, dizziness, and even tinnitus</span>.

For seniors, the challenge is compounded by age-related loss of muscle elasticity. Once the SCM hardens, it takes much longer to recover.

## 💆 How to Do the 30-Second Technique

**The Basic Technique:**
1. Look in a mirror and slowly turn your head to one side. Identify the prominent muscle running from below your ear to your collarbone.
2. Gently grasp that muscle between your thumb and index finger.
3. Starting from the top, slowly work your fingers downward for <mark>30 seconds</mark>.
4. Repeat on the other side.

**Important Cautions:**
- Use only gentle pressure — the sensation should be pleasantly uncomfortable, never painful
- Do not apply strong pressure over the carotid artery
- Stop immediately if you feel any pain or dizziness
- Those with high blood pressure should consult a doctor first

Practitioners report <span class="hl-blue">noticeable improvement in neck mobility within one to two weeks</span>.

## 🧓 The Smartphone Neck Epidemic Among Seniors

Smartphone ownership among those over 60 has grown dramatically — usage time has more than tripled over the past decade. The physics are unforgiving:

| Head Angle | Load on Neck Muscles |
|------------|---------------------|
| Upright (0°) | ~5–6 kg |
| 15° forward | ~12 kg |
| 30° forward | ~18 kg |
| 60° forward (typical phone use) | ~27 kg |

Seniors, whose muscles repair more slowly, accumulate this daily load — eventually reaching a point where the SCM barely relaxes at all.

## 🌸 Japan's Long Tradition of Caring for Kori

The Japanese word *kori* (こり) describes the specific sensation of muscular tension — a concept so embedded in Japanese daily life that it has centuries of associated healing practices. Japan's sentō (public bathhouse) culture, shiatsu, and acupuncture all predate Western medicine and remain widely practiced today.

In senior care facilities, neck and shoulder self-care is now integrated into *kaigo yobō* (介護予防, disability prevention) programs. Empowering older adults to care for their own bodies is recognized as one of the most effective paths to <span class="hl-blue">sustained independence</span>.
