**Hearing loss** progresses silently — and with every decibel lost, dementia draws closer. This stark warning from Associate Professor Akinori Kashio of the University of Tokyo Graduate School is resonating deeply with seniors across Japan.

## Key Takeaways

- 🔑 Untreated hearing loss increases <span class="hl-red">dementia risk</span> by up to <mark>5x</mark> according to research
- 🔑 A new eye-tracking app can assess cognitive function in just <mark>3 minutes</mark>, now certified internationally

## 🏥 Hearing Loss and Dementia — The Invisible Danger

"My hearing has gotten a bit worse." Many seniors feel this way but dismiss it as an inevitable part of aging. However, research by **Associate Professor Akinori Kashio** at the University of Tokyo reveals that this dismissal is the greatest danger of all.

When hearing loss reduces auditory stimulation to the brain, brain atrophy accelerates. The **temporal lobe** — the region responsible for memory and language — is particularly affected. The World Health Organization (WHO) has identified <span class="hl-red">hearing loss as the single largest modifiable risk factor for dementia</span>.

| Risk Factor | Dementia Risk Increase |
|------------|----------------------|
| Hearing loss (untreated) | Up to <mark>5x</mark> |
| Social isolation | Approx. <mark>1.6x</mark> |
| Physical inactivity | Approx. <mark>1.4x</mark> |

> "Getting hearing aids early is the first step in protecting your brain" — Prof. Akinori Kashio

The critical action is visiting an ENT specialist at the first signs of difficulty hearing. In Japan, hearing aid adoption stands at just <mark>14%</mark>, dramatically lower than the <mark>30-40%</mark> seen in Europe and the US. Cultural stigma around wearing hearing aids is delaying life-critical decisions.

## 💡 3 Minutes to Know — Eye-Tracking Opens a New Frontier in Early Detection

Another breakthrough is an <span class="hl-blue">eye-tracking app for early dementia detection</span>. Simply by watching video on a screen for <mark>3 minutes</mark>, the app analyzes cognitive function through eye movements.

What makes this app revolutionary is its language independence. Whether someone speaks Japanese, Chinese, or any other language, eye movements alone provide the assessment. The technology has already received international certification and is beginning its global rollout.

Traditional cognitive tests — involving questionnaires and drawing exercises — typically take <mark>30+ minutes</mark> and cause significant stress. Many seniors refuse to take them altogether. Eye-tracking removes that barrier entirely.

## 🌸 Dementia Prevention Cafes — A Small Revolution in Azamino

At a cafe near Azamino Station in Yokohama's Aoba Ward, "Dr. Keiko's Dementia Prevention Cafe" meets every third Thursday of the month. Over coffee, participants learn about dementia accurately and share their concerns in a supportive environment.

These <span class="hl-blue">community-based prevention initiatives</span> are spreading across Japan. Medical professionals come to neighborhoods, lowering barriers to early detection and intervention.

Japan's dementia population is estimated at <mark>7 million as of 2025</mark> — roughly 1 in 5 people aged 65 and older. Yet early intervention can delay onset and slow progression significantly.

## 🙏 Lessons for All of Us

Before accepting that "aging is inevitable," consider what is within your power. If a single hearing aid can protect your brain, it may be the simplest dementia prevention available. And if a 3-minute eye test becomes widespread, the tragedy of "it was too late when we noticed" could become a thing of the past.

What matters most is **knowing, and then acting**. The seniors gathering at Azamino's cafe teach us something profound: don't carry your fears alone — face the future together with your community.
