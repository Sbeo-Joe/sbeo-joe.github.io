<mark>60s</mark>. One fall. A broken femur. Surgery, followed by extended bed rest — and from there, a slide into permanent care dependency. This is not a distant hypothetical. Every year in Japan, hundreds of thousands of people live through exactly this scenario. Dr. Hideki Wada, one of Japan's foremost authorities on geriatric medicine, has a stark warning about how to break this chain before it starts.

## Key Takeaways

- The leading cause of falls among the elderly is obstacles on the floor inside their own homes
- What Dr. Wada recommends decluttering includes not just physical objects but mental assumptions
- Preventing osteoporosis can dramatically reduce the risk of fall-related fractures

## 🚨 Falls: The Biggest Risk Factor in Later Life

Approximately one in three people <mark>aged 65 and over</mark> experience at least one fall per year in Japan, and around 10% result in a fracture. The most dangerous outcome is a proximal femoral fracture near the hip joint. <span class="hl-red">Data shows that the mortality rate within one year can reach 20 to 30%.</span> Among survivors, roughly half struggle to regain independent walking ability.

> If you do not fall, you can stay healthy and active even at 80. Fall prevention is the greatest longevity strategy there is.

## 🏠 The First Thing to Declutter: Hazards Inside the Home

Dr. Wada recommends people in their <mark>60s</mark> address things on the floor of their own home first. Japanese homes are filled with fall risks: tatami mat edges, interior steps, loose slippers, power cords.

The most dangerous spots:
- **Entrance steps**: Losing balance while putting on shoes is extremely common
- **Hallways turned into storage**: A temporarily placed box becomes a weapon
- **Bathrooms and toilets**: Wet floors and poor lighting are prime fall conditions
- **Bedroom perimeter**: Many falls happen when getting up in the night
- **Staircases**: Stairs without handrails are a <span class="hl-red">leading fall site for people over 60</span>

| High-Risk Location | Priority | Recommended Action |
|-------------------|----------|--------------------|
| Bathroom/Toilet | High | Install grab bars, non-slip mats |
| Entrance | High | Eliminate step, add seating |
| Hallway/Living room | Medium | Clear all floor items |
| Staircase | High | Install handrails on both sides |
| Bedroom | Medium | Nightlights, adjust bed height |

## 💡 Decluttering Your Assumptions — Dr. Wada's Deeper Insight

Dr. Wada insists it is not just things that need to be discarded — assumptions too.

"My legs are just weaker with age." "A fall is no big deal." "Grab bars make me look old." — These **mental assumptions are the most dangerous things to hold onto**, the doctor argues.

Long-term care insurance in Japan covers up to <mark>200,000 yen</mark> for home modifications, yet many people in their <mark>60s and 70s</mark> decline, saying it is too early. But the right time to install a grab bar is before you fall, not after.

<span class="hl-blue">Preventive home modifications can reduce the risk of falls by 50% or more.</span>

## 🦴 Protecting Your Bones Means Protecting Your Life

Japan has an estimated <mark>12.8 million</mark> osteoporosis patients, approximately 80% of whom are women. But men cannot afford complacency — osteoporosis in men over <mark>70</mark> is rising sharply.

Steps to prevent osteoporosis:
- **Calcium intake**: Dairy, small fish, tofu, leafy greens — aim for <mark>700mg or more per day</mark>
- **Vitamin D**: <mark>15 to 30 minutes</mark> of sunlight daily enables natural synthesis
- **Walking**: Weight-bearing exercise maintains bone density
- **Quit smoking, limit alcohol**: Both significantly reduce bone density
- **Bone density screening**: Get regular screenings starting in your <mark>50s</mark>

## 🌟 From Defensive Aging to Radiant Aging

With a body and home that will not let you fall, you can still travel in your <mark>80s</mark>, pursue hobbies in your <mark>90s</mark>, walk in the park with grandchildren, and share meals with old friends. **The purpose of decluttering is to claim a richer future.**

<span class="hl-blue">Maintaining independence for as long as possible — this is the vision of later life most cherished by Japan's senior generation,</span> and small preventive actions are what turn that vision into reality.

Take a look around your home today. If there is something on the floor, clearing it away might just be the first step toward the old age you have always hoped for.
