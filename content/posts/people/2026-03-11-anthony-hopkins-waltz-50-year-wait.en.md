Sir Anthony Hopkins composed a waltz at <mark>26</mark>. He was a struggling actor then, more uncertain about the future than certain of it. He put the music away. Then life took over — and nearly fifty years passed.

## Key Takeaways

- Hopkins composed "And the Waltz Goes On" at age <mark>26</mark> and kept it in a drawer for nearly five decades before conductor André Rieu arranged its first full orchestral performance
- Hopkins, now <mark>88</mark>, was in the audience when his composition was finally performed — hearing his own piece brought to life for the first time
- The story is a quiet reminder that some creative work exists on its own timescale — and that what gets set aside is not necessarily lost

## A First Love Hidden Away

Most people know Anthony Hopkins from his performances. Hannibal Lecter. Richard Nixon. Odin. Two Academy Awards. A body of work that spans more than half a century of cinema and theatre. What fewer people know is that before all of that, there was music.

Hopkins was born in Port Talbot, Wales, in 1937, into a family of bakers. From childhood, music was his first and most instinctive passion. He taught himself piano and composed from an early age, finding in music a channel for something that words and action hadn't yet given him a way to express. Acting came later — and for decades, it consumed everything.

The waltz he composed at 26 went into a drawer. He carried it there for nearly half a century. Through two marriages, a well-documented battle with alcohol in the 1970s, relocation to America, the ascent to Hollywood stardom, and the accumulation of every professional honor his industry could offer, the waltz sat unperformed. It did not disappear. It waited.

## The Moment It Found a Voice

The piece came eventually to the attention of **André Rieu** — the Dutch conductor and violinist famous for making classical music accessible to vast popular audiences. Rieu arranged for "And the Waltz Goes On" to receive a full orchestral performance. When Hopkins was told the piece would finally be played by a professional orchestra, he responded simply: he wanted to hear it.

At <mark>88</mark>, Sir Anthony Hopkins sat in the audience and listened to a piece of music he had written when he was 26. The weight of those decades — the achievements, the struggles, the long arc of a life lived fully and sometimes dangerously — was present in that room. The composition was not sophisticated by the standards of classical repertoire. It was something more personal than sophisticated: it was honest. It was the work of a young man who felt something deeply and wrote it down.

> "I wrote it when I was young and hopeful about many things. I never thought anyone would play it. I'm glad they did."

## Why This Story Carries Weight

The arc of this story is not about delayed success in the conventional sense. Hopkins did not need the waltz to be performed in order to feel complete. He had built a life remarkable in its scope and depth without it. The waltz's performance at the end of his life is not the resolution of an incompleteness. It is something quieter: the return of something real.

In a culture obsessed with speed — instant publishing, viral moments, continuous output, the pressure to produce and perform at all times — Hopkins' story offers a different kind of permission. Some creative work exists on its own timescale. A piece of music written at 26 does not need to be performed at 27 to be real or to matter. The composition was complete the moment it was written. The performance, five decades later, was not its validation. It was its reunion.

For anyone who has set something aside — a piece of writing, a musical idea, a creative project that felt too uncertain or too personal to share — the story carries a specific kind of permission. The drawer is not the end of the story. It is part of it.

## The Full Life That Contained the Waltz

Hopkins has spoken candidly in interviews about the years when acting and personal struggle left little room for anything else. The alcohol that almost cost him his career in the 1970s. The discipline of recovery. The second act — artistically and personally — that followed.

What the waltz story suggests is that the creative impulse that drove Hopkins to compose at 26 never disappeared. It went underground, the way many things do in the midst of a demanding life. The capacity that made him a great actor — deep attentiveness to emotional truth, patience with complexity, willingness to inhabit difficult interior states — is the same capacity that produced the waltz. It is not surprising that the same person did both. It is surprising only that it took this long to become visible.

At <mark>88</mark>, Hopkins is still working. Still composing. Still capable, apparently, of being surprised — including by his own life. The waltz performance was not an ending. It felt more like a recognition.
